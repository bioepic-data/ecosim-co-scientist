# Resistance of microbial and soil properties to warming treatment seven years after boreal fire

## Study Citation and Significance

**Citation:** Allison, S.D., McGuire, K.L., & Treseder, K.K. (2010). Resistance of microbial and soil properties to warming treatment seven years after boreal fire. *Soil Biology & Biochemistry*, 42, 1872-1878.

**Study Significance:**
This study is **critically important** for understanding temporal dynamics of warming responses across post-fire succession in boreal forests. Unlike most warming studies that show positive responses, this paper documents **RESISTANCE** to warming 7-9 years after wildfire, contrasting sharply with an earlier study at the same site showing positive responses 1-3 years post-fire (Bergner et al. 2004).

**Key Contribution:** Demonstrates that positive feedbacks between climate warming and soil C loss may be **TRANSIENT** in permafrost-free boreal forests, with warming responses varying across successional gradients. This challenges assumptions of sustained warming responses and has major implications for modeling 45-60% of the boreal zone that lacks permafrost.

## Site Description

### Location and Climate
- **Site:** Fort Greely military base, Delta Junction, Alaska
- **Coordinates:** 63°55'N, 145°44'W
- **Ecosystem:** Boreal forest recovering from 1999 wildfire (7 years post-fire at experiment start)
- **Fire history:** Severe 1999 wildfire killed all canopy trees; removed ~60% of surface organic C
- **Permafrost:** Discontinuous in region; **NOT present at site**
- **Climate:**
  - Mean annual temperature: -2°C
  - Mean annual precipitation: 303 mm yr⁻¹
  - Dry and cold continental climate

### Soils
- **Classification:** Well-drained silt-loam
- **Parent material:** Gravelly glacial till and outwash
- **Horizon structure:**
  - Shallow organic horizon: <5 cm depth (reduced from pre-fire conditions)
  - Mineral A horizon: Silt-loam texture
- **Fire impacts:**
  - 60% of surface organic C removed by combustion
  - Unburned forest floor: 0-3 cm thick where present
  - Char and ash present immediately post-fire, but mostly gone by 2006

### Vegetation
**Successional stage:** Early-successional (7-9 years post-fire)

**Dominant plant taxa:**
- **Trees:** *Populus tremuloides* (trembling aspen), *Betula* spp. (birch), *Salix* spp. (willow)
- **Grasses:** *Calamagrostis* spp., *Festuca* spp.
- **Shrubs:** *Ledum* spp. (Labrador tea), *Vaccinium* spp. (blueberry)
- **Forbs:** *Lupinus* spp.

**Pre-fire vegetation:** *Picea mariana* (black spruce) forest with moss understory

**Successional trajectory:**
- 0-few years: Grasses and shrubs dominate
- 15-50 years: Deciduous *Populus tremuloides* dominates canopy
- >80 years: Black spruce (*Picea mariana*) returns to dominance

## Experimental Design

### Treatment Structure
- **Design:** Paired plot design with 5 experimental blocks
- **Plot size:** 2.5 m × 2.5 m
- **Spatial arrangement:** Control plot within 10 m of warmed plot in each pair
- **Duration:** 3 growing seasons (2006-2008)

### Warming Method: Greenhouse Passive Warming
**Construction:**
- A-frame structure constructed of two-by-fours
- Clear 6 mil plastic sheeting (AT Plastics, Inc)
- Plastic transmits 60-70% of photosynthetically active radiation
- Gaps between plastic and frame/ground allowed air circulation

**Treatment application:**
- **Growing season only:** Mid-May to mid-September each year (starting 2006)
- **Winter:** Plastic removed; snowfall could reach plots
- **Precipitation management:** Gutters collected precipitation; redistributed via tubing to 8 locations ≥50 cm apart

**Temperature effects:**
- **Soil warming achieved:** +1.20°C at 5 cm depth (p<0.05)
- **Diurnal temperature range:** ~15°C
- **Mean daily temperature range:** 5°C to 21°C across growing season
- **Temperature extremes:** Minimum ~1°C; maximum >30°C

**Chamber effects:**
- No chamber control (frames without plastic)
- Prior study at site showed no chamber effect (Bergner et al. 2004)
- Potential artifacts: Light attenuation, altered water balance

### Soil Moisture Effects
**Warming resulted in significant soil drying:**
- **Overall effect:** Significant reduction in gravimetric water content (warming × date interaction, p<0.05)
- **Specific dates with significant drying:**
  - August 21, 2006: p<0.001
  - August 14, 2007: p<0.05
  - September 17, 2007: p<0.01
- **Magnitude:** Up to 30% reduction in water content observed

**Moisture limitations assessment:**
- Lowest gravimetric moisture (~0.35 g g⁻¹ dry soil) corresponds to:
  - Volumetric moisture: ~0.25 cm³ cm⁻³ (assuming bulk density 0.69 g cm⁻³)
  - Soil matric potential: ~-0.1 MPa (for silt-loam)
  - **ABOVE** the -0.5 MPa threshold reported to constrain soil respiration
- **Conclusion:** Drying probably **NOT sufficient** to limit microbial activity

## Methods

### Soil CO₂ Efflux
**Measurement technique:**
- **Instrument:** PP Systems EGM-4 infrared gas analyzer with opaque chambers
- **Chamber dimensions:** 25 cm diameter
- **Installation:** Chamber bases inserted 2 cm into soil (May 2006)
- **Measurement protocol:**
  - Opaque lid placed on chamber base
  - Rate of CO₂ increase measured over 5-10 minutes
  - Soil temperature measured next to chamber with probe
- **Calculation:** Ideal gas law (chamber volume, ambient temperature, 1 atm pressure)
- **Frequency:** 3-4 time points per growing season (2006-2008)
- **Vegetation handling:**
  - Litter, grasses, mosses generally left in chambers
  - Aboveground biomass clipped August 21, 2006 (no significant effect on efflux)

### Nutrient Availability
**Resin bag method:**
- **Resin types:** Anion-exchange and cation-exchange bags
- **Installation:** 4 anion + 4 cation bags per plot, 5 cm depth, ≥25 cm apart
- **Deployment periods:**
  - Set 1: May 14 - September 17, 2006
  - Set 2: May 11 - September 19, 2007
- **Processing:**
  - Rinse in deionized water
  - Extract in 0.1 M HCl / 2.0 M NaCl
- **Analyses:**
  - **NH₄⁺:** Modified Berthelot-salicylate method (cation extracts)
  - **NO₃⁻:** Vanadium method (anion extracts)
  - **PO₄³⁻:** Malachite green method (anion extracts)
- **Units:** ng nutrient (N or P) g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹

### Microbial Biomass
**Chloroform fumigation-direct extraction:**
- **Sampling:** July 2006 soils (collected for enzyme assays)
- **Method:** K₂SO₄ extraction before and after chloroform fumigation
- **Efficiency factors:**
  - Microbial C: 0.45
  - Microbial N: 0.54
- **Measurements:**
  - Microbial biomass C (μg g⁻¹ soil)
  - Microbial biomass N (μg g⁻¹ soil)
  - Microbial C:N ratio

### Extracellular Enzyme Activities
**Sampling:**
- **Frequency:** 7 time points during 2006-2007 growing seasons
- **Protocol:** 3-5 soil cores per plot (5 cm depth, 2.5 cm diameter)
- **Processing:** Cores combined and homogenized by hand

**Colorimetric assays:**
All assays in 50 mM sodium acetate buffer, pH 5.0

1. **β-glucosidase** (cellulose degradation):
   - Substrate: 5 mM p-nitrophenyl-β-glucopyranoside
   - Product: p-nitrophenol (yellow)

2. **N-acetyl-glucosaminidase** (chitin degradation):
   - Substrate: 2 mM p-nitrophenyl-β-N-acetylglucosaminide
   - Product: p-nitrophenol (yellow)

3. **Glycine aminopeptidase** (protein/polypeptide degradation):
   - Substrate: 5 mM glycine p-nitroanilide
   - Product: p-nitroaniline (yellow)

4. **Polyphenol oxidase** (lignin degradation):
   - Substrate: 50 mM pyrogallol + 50 mM EDTA
   - Product: Colored oxidation product

**Assay protocol:**
- 150 μl substrate + 50 μl soil slurry
- Incubation: 1-4 hours
- Detection: Microplate reader
- **Units:** μmol product g⁻¹ dry soil h⁻¹

### Fungal Community Analysis
**Sampling and DNA extraction:**
- **Sampling date:** May 29, 2008
- **Protocol:** 3 soil samples composited per plot
- **Storage:** Frozen at -20°C
- **DNA extraction:** PowerSoil DNA kit (Mo Bio Laboratories) within 1 month
- **Pooling:** DNA from three 0.25 g subsamples

**PCR amplification:**
- **Primers:**
  - ITS-F (forward)
  - TW13 (reverse)
- **Target:** ITS and 28S regions of fungal rDNA
- **Amplicon size:** ~1200 bp
- **PCR conditions:**
  - Initial denaturation: 5 min at 95°C
  - 35 cycles: 30s at 95°C, 45s at 50°C, 6 min at 72°C
  - Final elongation: 10 min at 72°C

**Clone library construction:**
- **Vector:** pCR2.1-TOPO (Invitrogen TOPO TA Cloning kit)
- **Clone sampling:** 50 clones per plot
- **Total libraries:** 10 (5 control + 5 warmed)
- **Sequencing:** Bi-directional sequencing (Agencourt Bioscience)

**Sequence analysis:**
- **Assembly:** CodonCode Aligner (forward + reverse reads)
- **Alignment region:** ~425 bp segment of 28S rRNA gene
- **Alignment tool:** CLUSTALW
- **Quality control:** Removed poorly-aligned, low-quality, and non-fungal sequences
- **Total sequences:** 433 sequences (426 in alignment + 7 high-quality ITS-only)
- **Distance matrix:** DNADIST (Phylip package)
- **OTU assignment:** DOTUR (80-100% similarity cutoffs)
- **Total OTUs:** 113 OTUs at 99% similarity
- **Community analysis:**
  - Non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMS)
  - Multi-response permutational procedures (MRPP)
- **GenBank accession numbers:** GU211934-GU212366

### Statistical Analyses
**Repeated-measures ANOVA:**
- **Model:** SAS PROC MIXED
- **Factors:**
  - Block: Random factor
  - Warming treatment: Fixed effect
  - Date: Repeated factor
- **Covariance structure:** First-order autoregressive or compound symmetry (lowest AIC)
- **Post-hoc tests:** SLICE option for within-date treatment effects

**Transformations:**
- Temperature: Square-root (daily means)
- Glycine aminopeptidase: Square-root
- Polyphenol oxidase: Square-root
- All other data (except C:N): Log-transformed
- Purpose: Improve normality

**Paired t-tests:**
- Microbial C and N pools

**Multiple regression:**
- **Response:** Log-transformed CO₂ efflux
- **Predictors:** Soil temperature (probe data), log-transformed soil water content
- **Sample sizes:**
  - All data: n=69
  - Warmed plots only: n=35
  - Control plots only: n=34
- **Variable selection:** Stepwise (p<0.05 to enter and stay)
- **Confirmation:** Simple regression with all temperature data (n=99)

## Major Findings Summary

### SURPRISING LACK OF WARMING RESPONSE

Despite achieving **1.2°C soil warming**, the study found **MINIMAL SUPPORT** for the hypothesis that warming would stimulate microbial activity. This contrasts sharply with:
1. Meta-analysis showing increased CO₂ efflux and N mineralization with warming in most studies
2. **Prior study at same site** (Bergner et al. 2004): 20% increase in soil respiration with only 0.9°C warming (1-3 years post-fire)

### 1. Soil CO₂ Efflux: NO WARMING EFFECT

**Overall ANOVA results:**
- **Warming treatment effect:** NOT significant
- **Date effect:** Highly significant (p<0.001) - seasonal variation
- **Warming × Date interaction:** NOT significant

**Mean CO₂ efflux rates:**
- **Control plots:** 68 ± 4 mg CO₂-C m⁻² h⁻¹
- **Warmed plots:** Similar (no significant difference)

**Temperature and moisture relationships (multiple regression, n=69):**
- **Soil temperature:** Explained 54% of variance (p<0.001) - POSITIVE relationship
- **Soil moisture:** Explained only 6% of variance (p<0.01) - POSITIVE relationship
- **Simple regression (all data, n=99):**
  - CO₂ efflux increases exponentially with temperature (p<0.001, R²=0.49)
  - **BUT:** No difference in efflux between warmed and control treatments

**Within-treatment regressions:**
- **Control plots:** Both temperature and moisture significant (p<0.01)
- **Warmed plots:** Temperature significant (p<0.01), moisture NOT significant
- **Slope comparison:** Temperature relationship did NOT differ between treatments

**Interpretation:**
- CO₂ efflux responds to ambient temperature variation
- **BUT:** Warming treatment did NOT shift the baseline respiration rate
- Suggests **ACCLIMATION** or **CONSTRAINT** on warming response

### 2. Nutrient Availability: NO WARMING EFFECTS

**Resin-available NH₄⁺:**
- **Control:** 56 ± 30 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 69 ± 26 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Resin-available NO₃⁻:**
- **Control:** 3.4 ± 1.2 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 3.9 ± 1.8 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Effect:** NOT significant
- **Note:** Order of magnitude lower than NH₄⁺

**Resin-available PO₄³⁻:**
- **Control:** 77 ± 29 ng P g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 89 ± 49 ng P g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Interpretation:**
- NO evidence for warming-stimulated nutrient mineralization
- Contrasts with expectations from most warming studies

### 3. Microbial Biomass: NO WARMING EFFECTS

**Microbial biomass C (July 2006):**
- **Control:** 349 ± 81 μg g⁻¹ soil
- **Warmed:** 358 ± 34 μg g⁻¹ soil
- **Effect:** NOT significant (paired t-test)

**Microbial biomass N (July 2006):**
- **Control:** 26.7 ± 8.8 μg g⁻¹ soil
- **Warmed:** 25.8 ± 2.5 μg g⁻¹ soil
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Microbial C:N ratio:**
- **Control:** 15.5 ± 1.8
- **Warmed:** 14.1 ± 1.2
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Interpretation:**
- NO evidence for warming effects on microbial biomass pools
- Surprising given that greater mineralization typically supports more biomass

### 4. Extracellular Enzyme Activities: SMALL INCREASES

**β-glucosidase (cellulose degradation):**
- **Control:** 1.38 ± 0.08 μmol pNP g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 1.59 ± 0.09 μmol pNP g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Effect:** **+15% increase**, significant (p<0.05)
- **Within-date effects:** Only significant on July 2, 2006 (p<0.05)

**N-acetyl-glucosaminidase (chitin degradation):**
- **Control:** 0.54 ± 0.04 μmol pNP g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 0.73 ± 0.06 μmol pNP g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Effect:** **+35% increase**, highly significant (p<0.01)
- **Within-date effects:** NO significant effects on individual dates

**Glycine aminopeptidase (protein degradation):**
- **Control:** 0.513 ± 0.070 μmol pNA g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 0.376 ± 0.054 μmol pNA g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Polyphenol oxidase (lignin degradation):**
- **Control:** 15.3 ± 1.3 μmol pyrogallol g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Warmed:** 13.1 ± 1.1 μmol pyrogallol g⁻¹ soil h⁻¹
- **Effect:** NOT significant

**Interpretation:**
- **ONLY** cellulose- and chitin-degrading enzymes responded to warming
- Increases were SMALL and did NOT translate to increased CO₂ efflux or nutrient pools
- **Disconnect** between enzyme activities and ecosystem-level processes

### 5. Fungal Community Composition: NO WARMING EFFECTS

**Sequence summary:**
- **Total sequences:** 433 sequences across 10 libraries
- **OTU richness:** 113 OTUs at 99% similarity

**Taxonomic composition (80% similarity cutoffs):**
1. **OTU 1 (Ascomycota):** 40% of sequences
2. **OTU 2 (Basidiomycota):** 33% of sequences
3. **OTUs 3 & 6 (Zygomycota):** 10% of sequences
4. **Other OTUs (unidentified):** <17% of sequences

**Warming effects:**
- **NMS ordinations:** NO significant shifts in community structure
- **MRPP analysis:** NO significant treatment effects
- **Conclusion:** Fungal community composition STABLE across treatments

**Interpretation:**
- Contradicts hypothesis that warming would favor Ascomycetes and Zygomycetes over Basidiomycetes
- Suggests community may require longer time to respond (cf. Rinnan et al. 2007: 15 years)

## Proposed Mechanisms for Warming Resistance

### 1. LABILE CARBON DEPLETION (Primary Hypothesis)

**Fire-related C losses:**
- 1999 wildfire removed **60% of surface organic C**
- Combustion preferentially consumed labile, easily-degradable substrates
- Left behind **RECALCITRANT C** with weak short-term temperature responses

**Post-fire elevated temperatures accelerated depletion:**
- **2002 (3 years post-fire):** Burned soils 5°C warmer than unburned at 11 cm depth
- **2006-2007 (7-9 years post-fire):**
  - Burned site: 13.1°C mean temperature (5 cm depth)
  - Nearby unburned site: 9.3°C mean temperature (5 cm depth)
  - **Difference: +3.8°C** sustained warming in burned site
- **Consequence:** Years of elevated metabolism may have depleted remaining labile C pools

**Evidence for C depletion:**
1. **Declining C stocks:** Trend toward decreasing soil C since 1999 fire
2. **Lower CO₂ efflux:**
   - Burned site: 68 ± 4 mg CO₂-C m⁻² h⁻¹
   - Nearby unburned site: 106 ± 9 mg CO₂-C m⁻² h⁻¹
   - **36% lower in burned site** suggests lower C availability

**Mechanism:**
- Labile C forms have STRONG temperature responses (high Q₁₀)
- Recalcitrant C forms have WEAK temperature responses (low Q₁₀)
- After depletion of labile pool, warming has little effect on remaining recalcitrant C

**Explains temporal pattern:**
- **Bergner et al. 2004 (1-3 years post-fire):** Positive warming response when labile C still available
- **Current study (7-9 years post-fire):** No warming response after labile C depleted

### 2. REDUCED BELOWGROUND PLANT INPUTS

**Greenhouse artifacts:**
- Light attenuation: Plastic transmits only 60-70% of PAR
- Possible reduction in photosynthesis → Less C allocation belowground

**Prior evidence:**
- Bergner et al. (2004) found **33% reduction in root biomass** with warming

**Mechanism:**
- Reduced root exudation and litter inputs
- Less labile C supply to microbes
- **Offset** any stimulation of microbial metabolism by warming

**Importance:**
- If root inputs declined enough, could explain lack of CO₂ efflux response
- BUT: Lack of enzyme and biomass responses suggests this is NOT sole explanation

### 3. SOIL MOISTURE CONSTRAINTS (Unlikely)

**Soil drying observed:**
- Up to 30% reduction in gravimetric water content
- Significant on 3 of 7 sampling dates

**Why moisture probably NOT limiting:**

1. **Soil water potential analysis:**
   - Lowest moisture: ~0.35 g g⁻¹ gravimetric
   - Corresponds to ~-0.1 MPa matric potential (silt-loam)
   - **Well above** -0.5 MPa threshold for microbial constraints

2. **Regression results:**
   - Moisture explained only 6% of CO₂ variance
   - NO significant moisture effect in warmed plots separately
   - Temperature relationships did NOT differ between treatments

3. **Depth considerations:**
   - Moisture measured only at surface (0-5 cm)
   - Deeper horizons likely less affected by drying
   - Substantial fraction of respiration from deeper soils

**Conclusion:** Drying probably NOT sufficient to constrain warming response

### 4. PHYSIOLOGICAL OR COMMUNITY ACCLIMATION

**Microbial adaptation possibilities:**
- Physiological mechanisms (enzyme kinetics)
- Shifts in community composition toward warm-adapted taxa

**Evidence AGAINST in this study:**
- NO changes in fungal community composition
- Enzyme activities increased (not decreased as expected for acclimation)

**Time considerations:**
- Fungal community shifts may require >2-3 years
- Example: Rinnan et al. (2007) found effects after 15 years in subarctic heath

## Comparison to Other Studies

### Contrast with Bergner et al. (2004) - Same Site

**Bergner et al. (2004) study:**
- **Timing:** 2000-2002 (1-3 years post-fire)
- **Treatment:** Open-top chambers
- **Warming magnitude:** 0.9°C
- **Results:**
  - **20% increase in soil respiration**
  - 33% reduction in root biomass
  - **POSITIVE** warming response

**Current study (Allison et al. 2010):**
- **Timing:** 2006-2008 (7-9 years post-fire)
- **Treatment:** Greenhouse structures
- **Warming magnitude:** 1.2°C (LARGER than Bergner)
- **Results:**
  - **NO increase in soil respiration**
  - Small enzyme increases only
  - **RESISTANCE** to warming

**Cannot be explained by:**
- Weaker warming (current study had MORE warming)
- Different methods (both used passive warming chambers)

**Temporal dynamics interpretation:**
- **Early post-fire (1-3 years):** Positive feedback (labile C available)
- **Mid-succession (7-9 years):** Resistance (labile C depleted)
- **Mature forest (>80 years):** Potential reversal? (Allison & Treseder 2008 found negative effects)

### Contrast with Mature Boreal Forest (Allison & Treseder 2008)

**Nearby unburned mature forest (Allison & Treseder 2008):**
- **Age:** Old-growth black spruce forest
- **Warming:** Similar greenhouse method
- **Results:**
  - Lower soil CO₂ efflux with warming
  - Sharp decline in microbial biomass
  - Minor fungal community shifts
  - **NEGATIVE** feedback (C sink)

**Comparison with current study:**
- Burned site (7-9 years): Resistance (no change)
- Unburned mature: Negative response (decreased efflux)
- **Different successional stages → Different warming responses**

### Contrast with Warming Meta-Analysis (Rustad et al. 2001)

**Rustad et al. (2001) meta-analysis:**
- Most studies: Increased soil CO₂ efflux with warming
- Most studies: Increased N mineralization
- **Only 2 boreal sites included** (limited representation)

**Current study contribution:**
- Adds important boreal forest data
- Shows **EXCEPTION** to general pattern
- Highlights importance of:
  - Disturbance history (fire)
  - Successional stage
  - Substrate availability
  - Permafrost presence/absence

### Implications for Boreal Zone

**Permafrost-free boreal forests:**
- Represent **45-60% of boreal zone** (based on forest extent and permafrost distribution)
- Warming responses may be:
  - Transient (not sustained)
  - Variable across succession
  - Weaker than permafrost systems

**Permafrost systems (for comparison):**
- Show sustained C losses with warming (Van Cleve et al. 1990, Schuur et al. 2008, 2009)
- Positive feedbacks more consistent
- Thawing releases previously frozen C (not depleted by prior decomposition)

**Fire frequency considerations:**
- Boreal fires: ~11.1 million ha yr⁻¹ in Asia and North America
- If fire frequency increases with climate change:
  - Larger fraction of landscape in early succession
  - Warming responses may be WEAKER than expected from mature forests
  - Need to understand responses across successional gradients

## EcoSIM Modeling Relevance

### Critical Insights for Model Development

**1. Successional Stage Must Be Represented**

**Challenge for current models:**
- Most ecosystem models assume UNIFORM warming response
- Reality: Responses vary dramatically across succession

**Key patterns to capture:**
- **Early post-fire (1-3 years):** Positive feedback
- **Mid-succession (7-9 years):** Resistance to warming
- **Mature forest:** Potential negative feedback

**EcoSIM implementation needs:**
- Track time-since-fire or successional age
- Parameterize different substrate pools that change with succession
- Allow warming responses to vary with pool composition

**2. Substrate Quality Constraints**

**Labile vs. recalcitrant C pools:**
- **Labile C:** High Q₁₀, strong temperature response, depleted by fire and elevated temperatures
- **Recalcitrant C:** Low Q₁₀, weak temperature response, dominates after fire

**Model requirements:**
- Multiple C pools with different temperature sensitivities
- Fire-induced transfers: Labile C → Combustion loss + Recalcitrant C enrichment
- Post-fire elevated temperatures accelerate labile C depletion

**Key process:** Substrate depletion can OVERRIDE temperature effects on microbial metabolism

**3. Disconnect Between Enzyme Pools and Ecosystem Fluxes**

**Empirical pattern:**
- Enzyme activities increased 15-35%
- **BUT:** NO corresponding changes in CO₂ efflux or nutrient mineralization

**Mechanistic interpretation:**
- Enzyme production ≠ Substrate decomposition
- **SUBSTRATE AVAILABILITY** is the limiting factor, not enzyme capacity

**EcoSIM implication:**
- Models should focus on substrate availability and quality
- Enzyme production alone is NOT sufficient to predict decomposition rates
- Need to couple enzyme pools with substrate accessibility

**4. Microbial Community Stability**

**Surprising finding:**
- NO changes in fungal community composition despite warming
- NO changes in microbial biomass

**Interpretation:**
- Community composition may NOT be primary driver of warming responses
- **Substrate dynamics** more important than community shifts (at least on 2-3 year timescales)

**Model simplification:**
- May not need complex microbial community dynamics for short-term predictions
- Focus on substrate availability and abiotic constraints

**5. Temperature-Moisture Interactions**

**Empirical patterns:**
- Temperature explained 54% of CO₂ variance
- Moisture explained only 6%
- Warming caused drying (up to 30% reduction)
- **BUT:** Drying was NOT sufficient to constrain respiration

**Threshold behavior:**
- Soil water potential > -0.5 MPa: Moisture NOT limiting
- Below threshold: Moisture becomes critical constraint

**Model needs:**
- Represent soil water potential, not just water content
- Include threshold functions for moisture limitations
- Couple warming effects to soil drying (energy balance)

**6. Fire Effects on Soil Properties**

**Long-term consequences of fire:**
- 60% reduction in surface organic C
- Elevated soil temperatures for 7-9+ years post-fire (+3.8°C)
- Changed substrate composition (more recalcitrant)
- Thin organic horizon (<5 cm vs >10 cm pre-fire)

**Modeling fire impacts:**
- Combustion: Remove portion of labile C and surface organic matter
- Post-fire warming: Increase decomposition rates for years
- Substrate quality shifts: Increase recalcitrant:labile ratio
- Albedo effects: Reduced surface organic matter → Higher soil temperatures

**7. Comparison to Permafrost vs Permafrost-Free Systems**

**Permafrost-free (this study):**
- Transient positive feedback (early post-fire)
- Mid-succession resistance
- Substrate depletion constrains response

**Permafrost systems:**
- Sustained positive feedbacks
- Thawing releases previously frozen C
- Less substrate depletion (C was frozen, not decomposing)

**Model discrimination needed:**
- Separate parameterizations for permafrost vs permafrost-free boreal
- Critical for predicting responses in 45-60% of boreal zone without permafrost

### Specific Parameterization Data

**Temperature relationships:**
- Soil temperature explained 54% of CO₂ variance (positive, exponential)
- Warming magnitude: +1.2°C
- Temperature range: 5-21°C mean daily, -1 to >30°C extremes

**Enzyme kinetics:**
- β-glucosidase: 15% increase with warming (1.38 → 1.59 μmol g⁻¹ h⁻¹)
- N-acetyl-glucosaminidase: 35% increase (0.54 → 0.73 μmol g⁻¹ h⁻¹)
- NO changes in peptidase or polyphenol oxidase

**Baseline rates:**
- Soil CO₂ efflux: 68 mg CO₂-C m⁻² h⁻¹ (burned site, 7-9 years post-fire)
- Microbial biomass C: 350 μg g⁻¹ soil
- Microbial biomass N: 26 μg g⁻¹ soil
- Microbial C:N: ~15

**Nutrient availability:**
- NH₄⁺: 60 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹
- NO₃⁻: 3-4 ng N g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹ (order of magnitude lower)
- PO₄³⁻: 80 ng P g⁻¹ resin d⁻¹

**Soil properties:**
- Texture: Silt-loam
- Bulk density: 0.69 g cm⁻³
- Organic horizon depth: <5 cm (reduced from >10 cm pre-fire)
- pH: ~5.0 (acidic)

### Model Testing Opportunities

**Key tests for EcoSIM:**

1. **Can model reproduce LACK of warming response 7-9 years post-fire?**
   - Given labile C depletion
   - Given small enzyme increases
   - Given stable microbial communities

2. **Can model reproduce TEMPORAL DYNAMICS across succession?**
   - Positive response 1-3 years post-fire (Bergner et al. 2004)
   - Resistance 7-9 years post-fire (this study)
   - Possible negative response in mature forest (Allison & Treseder 2008)

3. **Can model separate substrate vs. enzyme limitations?**
   - Enzyme increases WITHOUT flux increases
   - Substrate availability as primary control

4. **Can model reproduce temperature-CO₂ relationships?**
   - Strong ambient temperature effects (R²=0.49)
   - NO treatment effect on baseline rate
   - Exponential (Q₁₀-type) relationship

### Integration with Other Studies in Database

**Complementary ecosystems:**
- **Temperate forests:** Peterjohn 1994, Butler 2012 (mostly positive responses)
- **Arctic tundra:** Chapin 1995 (mixed responses)
- **Subarctic heath:** Hartley 1999 (positive responses)
- **Peatlands:** Weedon 2012 (positive organic-N responses)
- **Boreal post-fire:** Allison 2010 (RESISTANCE - unique)

**Unique contribution:**
- **ONLY study showing complete resistance to warming** in collection
- Demonstrates importance of disturbance history and succession
- Critical for parameterizing transient vs. sustained feedbacks

**Cross-study comparisons:**
- Fire effects on C pools and warming responses
- Substrate quality as universal control across ecosystems
- Enzyme-flux disconnects (also seen in other studies)

## Study Limitations and Uncertainties

### Experimental Artifacts

**1. Chamber effects:**
- **Light attenuation:** Plastic transmits only 60-70% of PAR
  - Could reduce photosynthesis and belowground C allocation
  - No chamber control (frames only) to quantify artifact
- **Altered precipitation patterns:**
  - Gutters collect and redistribute precipitation
  - May not perfectly mimic natural patterns
  - Could create localized dry or wet spots
- **Air circulation:**
  - Gaps allow air exchange, but imperfect
  - May alter humidity, CO₂ levels slightly

**2. Growing season only treatment:**
- Warming applied **ONLY** mid-May to mid-September
- Winter period: Plastic removed, no warming
- **Consequence:** Missed potential winter warming effects
  - Winter respiration: 10-20% of annual total
  - Snow depth and freeze-thaw dynamics not manipulated

**3. Treatment duration:**
- Only **3 years** of warming (2006-2008)
- Longer experiments sometimes show delayed responses
- Example: Fungal community changes took 15 years in subarctic heath (Rinnan et al. 2007)

**4. Timing of experiment start:**
- Warming began **7 years after fire** (2006)
- Artificial compared to gradual climate change affecting all successional stages
- Missed potential early-successional responses that Bergner et al. (2004) captured

### Measurement Limitations

**1. Microbial community:**
- **Fungi only** - no bacterial community data
- Bacteria important decomposers in boreal soils
- Community assessed only **once** (May 2008) - early in 3rd growing season
- Clone library sequencing: Lower resolution than modern techniques

**2. Soil CO₂ efflux:**
- Chambers include plant respiration (roots + mycorrhizae)
- Cannot separate autotrophic vs. heterotrophic contributions
- Clipping on one date had no effect, but:
  - Root respiration not eliminated
  - Single measurement may miss seasonal patterns

**3. Nutrient availability:**
- Resin bags: Integrative measure over ~4 months
- Cannot detect short-term pulses or fine-scale temporal dynamics
- Placement at 5 cm: May miss shallower or deeper dynamics

**4. Moisture measurements:**
- Surface soil only (0-5 cm)
- Deeper horizons contribute substantial respiration
- May underestimate moisture availability for microbial activity

### Interpretation Uncertainties

**1. Mechanism for resistance:**
- **Multiple hypotheses** proposed:
  - Labile C depletion (primary, most supported)
  - Reduced plant inputs (possible contributor)
  - Moisture constraints (unlikely based on analysis)
  - Acclimation (no evidence)
- Difficult to definitively separate contributions

**2. Generalizability:**
- **Single site** in interior Alaska
- Specific fire severity (60% organic C removal)
- Specific successional stage (7-9 years)
- Permafrost-free (discontinuous permafrost zone)
- May not apply to:
  - Different fire severities
  - Other boreal regions
  - Permafrost-affected soils
  - Different successional stages

**3. Substrate depletion inference:**
- **Indirect evidence:**
  - Declining C stocks (trend)
  - Lower CO₂ efflux vs. unburned site
  - Elevated post-fire temperatures
- **Direct measurements lacking:**
  - Labile C pool sizes
  - Substrate quality indices
  - Decomposition rates of specific pools

**4. Temporal dynamics:**
- Comparison between studies:
  - Bergner et al. 2004: Different methods, plots, timepoints
  - Cannot rule out spatial variability as partial explanation
- **Ideally:** Same plots tracked from 1 year to 10+ years post-fire

### Statistical Power

**1. Small sample size:**
- **n = 5 replicate blocks**
- Limits power to detect small effects
- Large variability in some measurements (e.g., nutrients, microbial biomass)

**2. Multiple comparisons:**
- Many response variables tested
- No adjustment for family-wise error rate
- Some "non-significant" results could be Type II errors (false negatives)

**3. Within-date tests:**
- Post-hoc slice tests for enzyme activities
- Often non-significant despite overall warming effects
- Could reflect low power or true temporal variability

### Missing Measurements

**1. Plant dynamics:**
- NO measurements of:
  - Aboveground productivity
  - Root biomass and turnover
  - Root exudation rates
  - Litter inputs
- Critical for understanding belowground C supply

**2. Soil C pools:**
- NO fractionation into:
  - Labile vs. recalcitrant pools
  - Particulate organic matter vs. mineral-associated
  - Different chemical fractions
- **Central to main hypothesis** but not directly tested

**3. N cycling processes:**
- NO measurements of:
  - Gross N mineralization and immobilization
  - Nitrification rates
  - N₂O emissions
  - Microbial N uptake
- Resin bags provide only NET availability

**4. Enzyme kinetics:**
- Potential activities measured, not:
  - In situ activities
  - Temperature responses (Q₁₀)
  - Substrate affinities (Km)
  - Maximum velocities (Vmax)

## Conclusions and Broader Implications

### Key Conclusions

1. **Post-fire boreal ecosystems show RESISTANCE to warming 7-9 years after fire**, contrasting with positive responses 1-3 years post-fire at the same site.

2. **Labile carbon depletion** is the most likely mechanism, resulting from:
   - Fire-induced combustion of easily degradable substrates
   - Years of elevated soil temperatures accelerating decomposition
   - Remaining substrates are recalcitrant with weak temperature sensitivity

3. **Enzyme activities can increase WITHOUT corresponding changes in ecosystem-level processes** (CO₂ efflux, nutrient availability), indicating that **substrate availability**, not enzyme capacity, limits decomposition.

4. **Fungal community composition remained stable**, suggesting that community shifts may not be necessary for warming responses (or may take >3 years).

5. **Positive feedbacks between warming and C mineralization may be TRANSIENT** in permafrost-free boreal forests, varying across successional gradients.

### Broader Implications

**For Climate-Carbon Cycle Feedbacks:**

1. **Boreal forest responses to warming are NOT UNIFORM:**
   - Vary with successional stage
   - Vary with disturbance history (fire)
   - Vary with permafrost presence/absence

2. **Permafrost-free boreal systems (45-60% of boreal zone):**
   - May show weaker and more variable warming responses
   - Positive feedbacks may not be sustained
   - Different from permafrost systems with sustained C losses

3. **Fire regime changes compound complexity:**
   - Increased fire frequency → More landscape in early succession
   - Early succession: Transient positive feedback
   - Mid-succession: Resistance
   - Net effect on regional C balance UNCERTAIN

4. **Substrate quality dynamics are critical:**
   - Overwhelm enzyme production and microbial metabolism as controls
   - Must be represented in models
   - Affected by fire, temperature history, inputs

**For Ecosystem Modeling:**

1. **Models must represent:**
   - Successional dynamics and time-since-disturbance
   - Multiple substrate pools with different temperature sensitivities
   - Fire effects on substrate quality and availability
   - Substrate depletion over time

2. **Simplified assumptions may be adequate for:**
   - Microbial community dynamics (at least short-term)
   - Enzyme kinetics vs. substrate availability

3. **Critical processes to capture:**
   - Labile C depletion trajectories
   - Post-fire elevated temperatures
   - Substrate quality shifts with fire and succession

**For Future Research:**

1. **Long-term experiments needed:**
   - Track same plots from 0 to >50 years post-fire
   - Capture full successional trajectory
   - Document temporal dynamics definitively

2. **Direct substrate measurements needed:**
   - Labile vs. recalcitrant C pools
   - Chemical fractionation
   - Decomposition rates of different pools
   - Temperature sensitivities (Q₁₀) of pools

3. **Mechanistic experiments:**
   - Substrate addition experiments
   - Separate fire effects from temperature effects
   - Test substrate depletion hypothesis directly

4. **Regional extrapolation:**
   - More sites across boreal zone
   - Different fire severities
   - Permafrost vs. permafrost-free
   - Different climate zones within boreal

5. **Integration of processes:**
   - Link plant productivity to belowground C supply
   - Couple fire ecology with C cycling
   - Connect successional vegetation change to soil dynamics

### Final Perspective

This study provides a **critical counterpoint** to the prevailing narrative of sustained positive feedbacks between warming and soil C loss. It demonstrates that **ecological context matters enormously** - disturbance history, successional stage, substrate availability, and permafrost presence/absence all modulate warming responses.

The **"resistance"** observed 7-9 years post-fire is not permanent, but represents a phase in ecosystem recovery where substrate depletion temporarily overrides temperature effects. This transient resistance has major implications for regional and global C cycle predictions, especially as fire regimes shift with climate change.

For **EcoSIM modeling**, this study highlights the absolute necessity of representing substrate quality dynamics and post-disturbance trajectories. Models that assume uniform, sustained warming responses will likely overestimate C losses from permafrost-free boreal forests, which comprise the majority of the boreal zone.
