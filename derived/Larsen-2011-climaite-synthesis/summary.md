## Study Site

**Location**: Brandbjerg, Denmark (55°53'N, 11°58'E) - SAME SITE as Andresen 2010

**Ecosystem Type**: Dry temperate heathland/grassland

**Soil**:
- Well-drained sandy deposit
- pH 4-5
- Organic top layer: 2-5 cm depth
- Nutrient-poor

**Climate**:
- Long-term mean annual temperature: 8.0°C
- Long-term mean annual precipitation: 613 mm

**Vegetation**:
- **Dominant species**: *Calluna vulgaris* (evergreen dwarf shrub, ~60% of aboveground N)
- **Co-dominant**: *Deschampsia flexuosa* (perennial C3 grass, ~30% of aboveground N)
- Various mosses and herbs

**Atmospheric N deposition**:
- 2006: 1.27 ± 0.07 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹
- 2007: 1.35 ± 0.04 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹

## Experimental Design

### Treatments (Full 2×2×2 factorial design)

**8 treatments × 6 replicates = 48 plots**

1. **Elevated CO₂**: 510 ppm (ambient ~385 ppm)
   - Method: FACE-style fumigation
   - Operation: Daytime only
   - Feedback control linked to wind

2. **Warming (T)**: Passive nighttime warming
   - Method: Curtains 50 cm above ground (automatic deployment)
   - Target: +1.5°C soil warming at 2 cm depth
   - **Achieved warming** (October 2005 - December 2007):
     - Daily maximum elevation: 1.5°C (5 cm soil), 1.8°C (surface), 3.2°C (20 cm air)
     - Seasonal ranges at 5 cm depth:
       - Spring: 0.6-1.5°C
       - Summer: 0.5-1.3°C
       - Autumn: 0.3-1.0°C
       - Winter: 0.2-0.7°C (lowest warming effect)

3. **Drought (D)**: Extended summer drought
   - Method: Automated rain-out shelters
   - Duration: ~1 month each summer
   - **2006**: 52 mm removed, 11% peak soil water reduction (3-20 July, 26 July-4 Aug)
   - **2007**: 94 mm removed, 13% peak soil water reduction (21 May-22 June)
   - Target: Stop when soil water ~5% v/v at 0-20 cm depth

**Plot Design**:
- 12 octagons (7 m diameter)
- Arranged in 6 blocks of 2 octagons (paired for CO₂)
- Each octagon: 4 treatment plots (9.2 m² each)

**Treatment combinations**:
A (ambient control), CO₂, D, T, DCO₂, TCO₂, TD, TDCO₂

### Measurements - Comprehensive N Cycle Assessment

This synthesis reports on **47 N-related variables** across the N cycle:

#### 1. Plant Aboveground Biomass and C/N Ratios

**Non-destructive biomass**: Point intercept method + regression
- Calluna, Deschampsia, mosses
- Normalized to account for pre-treatment differences

**C/N ratios and N concentrations** (August 2007 harvest):
- Calluna: Green leaves, flowers, green tissue, wood
- Deschampsia: Green leaves, whole plant, litter
- Mosses: Whole plant
- Litter N production (Deschampsia only)

#### 2. Belowground Pools (0-10 cm depth)

**Plant roots**: Calluna, Deschampsia
**Microbial biomass N**: Chloroform fumigation-extraction
**Soil extractable N**: NO₃⁻, NH₄⁺, DON
**Soil organic matter N (SOM-N)**

#### 3. Soil Fauna Biomass and Mineralization

**Sampling**: October-November 2007

**Groups measured**:
- **Protozoa**: Most Probable Number method, AS medium, 15°C growth
- **Nematodes**: Modified Baermann extraction (48 h)
- **Enchytraeids**: Maraldo et al. (2008) method
- **Microarthropods**: Holmstrup et al. (2007) method

**Biomass N calculations**:
- Protozoa and nematodes: Literature conversion factors (Stout & Heal 1967, Sohlenius & Sandor 1987)
- Enchytraeids: 11.5% N of dry weight
- Microarthropods: 10% N of dry weight

**N mineralization by fauna**:
- **Microfauna** (protozoa + bacterivorous nematodes):
  - Turnover rate: 0.16 day⁻¹ for both groups
  - Yield on bacteria: 0.4 (protozoa), 0.04 (nematodes)
- **Mesofauna**:
  - Enchytraeids: Annual turnover 1.2
  - Microarthropods: Annual turnover 1.8

**Total fauna N mineralization in ambient plots**: 5.0 ± 0.6 g NH₄⁺-N m⁻² yr⁻¹
- Comparable to bulk N deposition (1.35 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹)
- 71% of gross mineralization from isotope dilution

#### 4. Gross N Mineralization and Immobilization

**Method**: ¹⁵N isotope pool dilution (lab incubation)
**Sampling**: September 2007
- Soil cores: 0-5 cm and 5-10 cm depths
- Sieved, stored at 5°C for ~1 week

**Incubation procedure**:
- 10 g fresh soil in 250 mL bottles
- Addition: 400 μL ¹⁵N-NH₄Cl solution (5 atom% ¹⁵N, 5 μg N g⁻¹ soil)
- Temperature: 20°C
- Duration: 7 days
- Analysis: t=0 and t=7 days

**Extraction**: Microdiffusion method (Sørensen & Jensen 1991)
**Calculations**: Kirkham & Bartholomew (1954) equations

**Gross rates calculated**:
- Gross N mineralization
- NH₄⁺ consumption

**Upscaling to annual rates**: Q₁₀ function (Q₁₀ = 2) applied to mean daily soil temperature at 5 cm depth

#### 5. Potential Nitrification and Denitrification

**Sampling**: November 2007 (0-10 cm depth)
**Storage**: 4°C, analyzed within 24 h

**Potential nitrification**:
- Method: Modified Belser & Mays (1980), without chlorate
- Soil: 3 g samples
- Measurement: Increase in NO₂⁻ + NO₃⁻

**Potential denitrification**:
- Method: Wolsing & Prieme (2004)
- Soil: 10 g samples
- Measurement: Linear N₂O accumulation over 180 min

**Upscaling**: Same Q₁₀ approach as gross mineralization

#### 6. N₂O Emissions (In Situ)

**Method**: Static chamber
- Chambers: White PVC, 15 or 45 cm height (seasonal vegetation adjustment)
- Collars: 60×60 cm, permanently installed
- Enclosure time: 2-2.5 hours
- Headspace sampling: 4 times per enclosure
- Analysis: Gas chromatography
- Flux calculation: Linear regression

**Campaigns**:
- June 2006 - March 2007: 9 campaigns, all 8 treatments
- April - June 2007: 5 campaigns, 5 treatments only (A, CO₂, D, DCO₂, TDCO₂)
- Extrapolation for T, TD, TCO₂ in March-June 2007

**Annual cumulative fluxes**: Interpolation between measurement days

#### 7. N₂O Reductase Activity (Lab Assay)

**Sampling**: November 2007 soil cores
**Pre-treatment**: Nitrate removal (3× PBS wash, centrifugation)
**Incubation medium**: 0.5 mM glucose + 0.5 mM sodium acetate + 0.5 mM sodium succinate
**Setup**: 100 mL bottles, N₂ atmosphere, 100 ppm N₂O added
**Incubation**: 22°C, shaking (200 rpm)
**Measurements**: Headspace N₂O at 0, 1, 3, 6, 24 hours (GC analysis)

**Result**: Negligible N₂O reductase activity (<1 ng N g⁻¹ dw h⁻¹) → N₂ production assumed negligible

#### 8. Leaching and Bulk Deposition

**Bulk N deposition**:
- Collection: Monthly, 3 rain collectors (site level, n=3)
- Height: 2 m above soil surface
- Analysis: NO₃⁻, NH₄⁺, total N, DON

**Leachate water collection**:
- **5 cm depth**: Passive PVC collectors below organic layer
- **60 cm depth**: PTFE suction cups (continuous sub-atmospheric pressure)
- Frequency: Monthly
- Analysis: NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻, total N (UV-persulphate oxidation), DON

**Water percolation modeling**:
- Model: CoupModel (Jansson & Moon 2001)
- Parameterization: Soil properties, on-site measurements
- Validation: Time series of soil temperature and moisture
- Output: Percolation below 60 cm

**N leaching calculation**: Percolation volume × concentration at 60 cm depth

**Key finding**: Leaching highly sensitive to precipitation timing
- Similar bulk deposition in both years
- Drastically different leaching (4× higher in wetter 2007)
- Suggests ecosystem close to **N saturation**

## Treatment Effects on N Cycle

### 1. Elevated CO₂ Effects

#### Aboveground (significant):
- **Increased C/N ratios** (P ≤ 0.05):
  - Calluna leaves: +17% (P=0.04)
  - Calluna flowers: +16% (P=0.02)
  - Deschampsia leaves: +16% (P=0.01)
  - Mosses: +44% (single treatment only, P=0.05, 3-way interaction P=0.003)
- **Decreased litter N concentration**:
  - Deschampsia litter: -24% (P=0.05)

**Interpretation**:
- Consistent with CO₂-induced photosynthetic downregulation (Albert 2009)
- Changes cascading from leaves → litter after 2 years
- Whole-plant C/N ratios not yet affected (too early)

#### Belowground (minimal direct effects):
- **No main effects** on mineralization, microbial biomass, or fauna
- But **reduced leachate concentrations** at 5 cm depth (single treatment analysis):
  - NO₃⁻-N: -76% (P=0.01)
  - DON: -21% (P=0.01)
- These effects disappeared in treatment combinations (antagonistic interactions)

**Plant biomass N**: No increase in Calluna or Deschampsia (contrary to expectation)

## Interaction Analysis - Major Innovation

### Frequency of Interactions

Across **47 N-cycle variables**:
- **Total significant interactions**: 15 (32% of variables)
- **Antagonistic interactions**: 13 (87% of interactions!)
- **Synergistic interactions**: 4 (27%)
  - Note: 2 three-way interactions had both types
- **Simple additive effects** (two main effects, no interaction): 3 (6%)

**Two-way interactions** (9):
- D×CO₂: 6 interactions
- T×CO₂: 5 interactions
- T×D: 3 interactions

**Three-way interactions** (T×D×CO₂): 6 interactions
- Potential nitrification (P=0.0002)
- Extractable DON (P=0.02)
- Moss C/N ratio (P=0.003)
- N₂O emissions (P=0.04)
- Leachate NO₃⁻ at 5 cm (P=0.002)
- Leachate DON at 5 cm (P=0.03)

### Dominance of Antagonism

**Definition of antagonism**: Combined effect led to reduction of observed individual effects
**Definition of synergism**: Combined effect led to amplification OR significant effect only in combination

**Key examples of antagonism**:

1. **Moss C/N ratio**: Increased under D alone (+31%) and CO₂ alone (+44%), but unchanged when combined

2. **Gross mineralization**: Increased under T alone (+50%), but effect reduced in TCO₂ combination

3. **CO₂ effects on leachate**: Decreased NO₃⁻ and DON at 5 cm in single CO₂ treatment, but effect disappeared in combinations

4. **Warming + CO₂ on microbial N**: Antagonistic interaction for microbial N immobilization

**Implication**: **Single-factor experiments likely overestimate ecosystem responses**

### Comparison to Literature

**Barnard et al. (2005)** review of 25 multifactor studies:
- Found interactions "generally rare"
- But those studies dominated by CO₂ × N addition
- Strong N addition responses may hide weaker interactions

**Luo et al. (2008)** analysis of 7 multifactor studies:
- Three-way interactions rare
- Two-way interactions more common
- Positive interaction CO₂ × warming
- Negative interaction CO₂ × reduced precipitation

**This study's contribution**:
- **47 variables** (most comprehensive for single study)
- **Systematic categorization** of interaction types
- **32% of variables showed interactions** (not rare!)
- **6 three-way interactions** found (previous studies: rare or absent)
- **Antagonism dominates** (opposite pattern to Luo et al. for some interactions)

### Hypothesis: Antagonism in Multi-Factor Climate Change

**Based on observed pattern**:
> "We raise the hypothesis that combined effects of all future climate change are dampened compared with expected effects based on simple addition of single treatment responses."

**Implications**:
1. Single-factor studies likely overestimate responses
2. Multifactor experiments critical for realistic predictions
3. Ecosystems may be more robust to multiple stressors than expected
4. Need mechanistic understanding of why antagonism dominates

**Recommendation**: Test this hypothesis with multivariable approaches in other multifactor experiments

## Major Findings

### 1. Drought Effects Dominated After 2 Years

Of three climate drivers:
- **Drought**: 11 significant main effects
- **Warming**: 6 significant main effects
- **Elevated CO₂**: 4 significant main effects

### 2. CO₂ Effects on Plant Stoichiometry

- Increased C/N ratios in photosynthetic tissues (both dominant species)
- Effect beginning to cascade to litter N concentration
- Combined with increased tannin concentrations (Schmidt et al. 2007)
- Likely to affect litter decomposition rates in longer term
- Consistent with **Progressive Nitrogen Limitation (PNL)** framework (Luo et al. 2004)

### 3. Warming Increased N Turnover

- Stimulated all measured N transformation processes:
  - Gross mineralization
  - NH₄⁺ consumption
  - Potential nitrification
  - Potential denitrification
  - N₂O emissions (with interactions)
- First field demonstration of warming effect on N₂O (previous studies: Peterjohn et al. 1994, Mchale et al. 1998 found no effect)

### 4. Drought Reduced N Turnover

- Reduced gross mineralization
- Reduced fauna biomass (enchytraeids, protozoans) - see Maraldo et al. 2010
- Reduced fauna N mineralization
- Reduced soil NH₄⁺ pool
- Reduced litter N production

**Mechanism**: Direct moisture limitation + indirect fauna population effects

### 5. Soil Fauna Critical to N Cycling

**Fauna N mineralization**: 5.0 ± 0.6 g NH₄⁺-N m⁻² yr⁻¹
- **71% of gross N mineralization** from isotope dilution!
- 4× bulk N deposition
- Implies most bacterial mineralization facilitated by fauna predation
- Suggests ~75% of activity from bacteria/archaea, ~25% from fungi

**Fauna groups**:
- Nematodes: 2.63 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹ (53%)
- Protozoans: 1.58 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹ (32%)
- Enchytraeids: 0.59 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹ (12%)
- Microarthropods: 0.19 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹ (4%)

**Fauna biomass**: Only 10% of microbial biomass, but disproportionate effect on N cycling

### 6. Interactions Common, Antagonism Dominates

- **32% of 47 variables** showed significant interactions
- **Antagonistic:Synergistic ratio** ≈ 3:1
- Combined responses **smaller** than single-treatment responses
- Ecosystems more robust to multi-factor change than expected from single factors

### 7. Leaching Highly Sensitive to Precipitation Timing

- No treatment effects on leaching (after 2 years)
- But **4× higher leaching** in wet summer 2007 vs. dry summer 2006
- 46% of annual leaching in 3 wet summer months (2007)
- Suggests ecosystem near N saturation
- Future: More intense precipitation events → increased N losses

### 8. Effects Persisting in Full Treatment Combination

**Future climate scenario (TDCO₂)** shows:
- ✓ Increased C/N ratios in leaves (both species)
- ✓ Reduced Deschampsia litter N production
- ✓ Reduced fauna N biomass and mineralization

**Overall**: **Reduced N availability** likely in future climate

### 9. Potential for Progressive N Limitation

Despite current lack of clear N limitation:
- Increased plant tissue C/N ratios (reduced N demand per C fixed)
- Reduced litter N inputs
- Reduced fauna N mineralization
- Effects cascade from leaves → litter after only 2 years

**Conclusion**: PNL may constrain CO₂ growth response in longer term

## Implications for EcoSIM Modeling

### This Study Provides:

**Comprehensive N cycle dataset**:
- 47 variables across all N cycle components
- Plant (2 dominant species), microbial, fauna, soil pools
- Process rates: mineralization, nitrification, denitrification, N₂O
- Stoichiometry: C/N ratios across plant tissues
- Fluxes: Leaching, deposition, gaseous losses

**Multi-factorial treatment design**:
- 2×2×2 factorial (8 combinations)
- Allows separation of main effects and interactions
- Interaction quantification (antagonistic vs. synergistic)
- Tests model ability to reproduce emergent interactions

**High-quality measurements**:
- Isotope pool dilution (gross rates)
- PLFA analysis (microbial community - see Andresen 2010)
- Fauna biomass and activity
- Multiple depths (0-5, 5-10 cm for some variables)
- Multiple seasons (6 per year for some variables)

**Same site as Andresen 2010**:
- Can combine datasets for comprehensive validation
- Andresen: 6-month buried bag incubations (in situ, winter)
- Larsen: Annual synthesis with isotope dilution (lab, upscaled)
- Complementary approaches to same processes

**Site conditions**:
- Soil pH: 4-5
- Soil type: Sandy, nutrient-poor
- Organic layer: 2-5 cm
- Vegetation: Calluna (60%) + Deschampsia (30%) + mosses (10%)
- N deposition: 1.3 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹

**Treatment specifications**:
- Warming: +0.5-1.5°C (0-5 cm depth, seasonal variation)
- CO₂: 510 ppm vs. 385 ppm ambient
- Drought: Soil water 5% vs. 17% (0-20 cm), ~1 month summer

**Validation targets - Process rates** (ambient plots, g N m⁻² yr⁻¹):
- Gross N mineralization: 7.0 ± 1.0
  - 0-5 cm: 4.46 ± 0.42
  - 5-10 cm: 2.51 ± 0.59
- NH₄⁺ consumption: 3.36 ± 0.65 (0-5 cm)
- Fauna N mineralization: 5.0 ± 0.6
- Potential nitrification: 3.82 ± 1.63
- Potential denitrification: 19.0 ± 5.8
- N₂O emissions: 0.031 ± 0.006
- Total N leaching: 0.80 ± 0.37 (highly variable)

**Validation targets - Treatment responses**:

*CO₂ effects*:
- Calluna leaf C/N: +17%
- Deschampsia leaf C/N: +16%
- Deschampsia litter N conc: -24%

*Drought effects*:
- Gross mineralization: -8%
- Fauna N mineralization: -18 to -29%
- Deschampsia litter N production: -30%

*Warming effects*:
- NH₄⁺ consumption: +159%
- Potential nitrification: +51%

**Validation targets - Interactions**:
- Expect antagonistic interactions to dominate
- Combined treatment responses < sum of individual responses

### Model Testing Opportunities

1. **Can EcoSIM reproduce the dominance of antagonistic interactions?**
   - Are interactions emergent from model structure?
   - Or do they require explicit parameterization?

2. **Does model capture the critical role of soil fauna?**
   - Fauna responsible for 71% of gross mineralization
   - Drought effects mediated through fauna populations
   - Fauna turnover rates and yields

3. **Can model simulate cascading C/N ratio effects?**
   - CO₂ → increased leaf C/N → reduced litter N (2 year lag)
   - Will this cascade to altered decomposition rates?
   - Progressive N limitation over longer timescales?

4. **Does model capture warming stimulation of all N processes?**
   - Mineralization, nitrification, denitrification all increased
   - Temperature sensitivity (Q₁₀) appropriately parameterized?
   - Seasonal variation in warming effect (higher in growing season)

5. **Can model reproduce drought suppression of N cycling?**
   - Moisture limitation of microbial processes
   - Fauna population responses to drought
   - Threshold effects (drought stopped at 5% soil water)

6. **Does model predict interannual leaching variability?**
   - 4× difference between dry and wet year
   - Sensitivity to precipitation timing (summer vs. winter)
   - Can it capture the 46% of leaching in 3 wet summer months?

7. **Multi-depth dynamics**:
   - Different responses at 0-5 cm vs. 5-10 cm
   - Warming effects stronger at surface
   - Drought effects concentrated in top layer

## Data Quality and Limitations

### Strengths:
- **Comprehensive measurements** (47 N-cycle variables)
- **Full factorial design** (8 treatment combinations)
- **Good replication** (n=6 per treatment)
- **Multiple methods** for same processes (e.g., gross vs. net mineralization)
- **Multi-scale**: Processes, pools, stoichiometry, fluxes
- **Isotope pool dilution** (gold standard for gross rates)
- **Fauna measurements** (often neglected)
- **Published in high-impact journal** (Global Change Biology)
- **Part of larger CLIMAITE project** (multiple coordinated papers)
- **Detailed statistical analysis** of interactions

### Limitations:

**Methodological**:
- **Isotope dilution**: Lab incubation at constant 20°C
  - Upscaled to annual using Q₁₀ function (Q₁₀=2 assumed)
  - May not capture in situ conditions
  - Compare to Andresen 2010 in situ buried bags for validation
- **Potential rates**: Nitrification and denitrification measured as potentials
  - May not reflect actual field rates
  - Provide maximum capacity, not realized rates
- **Fauna mineralization**: Calculated from biomass + literature turnover values
  - Few references for bacterivore parameters
  - Uncertainty in turnover rates and growth yields
  - But calculated value (5.0 g) matches well with isotope dilution (7.0 g)
- **Root biomass**: High spatial heterogeneity
  - Small samples may not be representative
  - Drought effect on Calluna roots uncertain

**Temporal**:
- **Short duration** (2 years of treatment)
  - Early transient responses may not persist
  - Longer-term trends uncertain
  - But effects already cascading (leaves → litter)
- **Limited seasonal coverage** for some variables
  - Most data from single time point (2007)
  - Isotope dilution: September only
  - Fauna: October-November only
  - Doesn't capture full seasonal dynamics

**Spatial**:
- **Single site** (no geographic replication)
- **Specific ecosystem type** (temperate heathland)
- **Specific soil** (acidic sandy, pH 4-5)
- Limits generalization to other ecosystems

**Treatment-specific**:
- **Passive warming**: Lower magnitude than active methods
  - Peak 1.5°C at 5 cm (daily average)
  - Seasonal variation (higher in growing season)
  - May underestimate warming responses
- **Drought**: No increased winter precipitation
  - Climate projections suggest wetter winters + drier summers
  - Current design: Drier summers only
  - Missing potential winter precipitation effects
- **CO₂**: FACE system
  - Excellent control, but limited spatial extent
  - Only 6 elevated CO₂ octagons

**Statistical**:
- **Multiple comparisons** (47 variables)
  - Some significant results may be Type I errors
  - But patterns consistent across related variables
- **Complex interactions**:
  - Some three-way interactions hard to interpret mechanistically
  - DLSM analysis helpful but adds complexity

### Uncertainty Sources:
- Spatial heterogeneity within plots (especially roots)
- Upscaling from lab incubations to annual field rates
- Fauna turnover rate estimates from literature
- Extrapolation of N₂O measurements (missing March-June for some treatments)
- Leaching calculation (modeled percolation × measured concentration)
- High interannual variability (precipitation-driven)

## Relevance to Warming/Nitrogen Meta-Analysis

**High relevance**:

1. **Multi-factorial climate experiment**:
   - Tests interactions between warming, drought, CO₂
   - Most comprehensive N cycle dataset from any multi-factor experiment
   - Directly relevant to understanding climate change effects

2. **Interaction quantification**:
   - Systematic analysis of 15 interactions
   - Framework for categorizing antagonistic vs. synergistic
   - Tests whether single-factor studies overestimate responses

3. **Warming effects on N cycling**:
   - Increased mineralization, nitrification, denitrification, N₂O
   - Quantified magnitude (+51% to +159% for different processes)
   - Demonstrates warming stimulates all N transformations

4. **Drought as modifier**:
   - Important for future climate scenarios
   - Demonstrates antagonistic W×D interactions
   - Fauna-mediated responses

5. **Progressive N limitation**:
   - Empirical evidence for PNL initiation
   - CO₂ → C/N → litter quality cascade (2 years)
   - Relevant to long-term C sequestration potential

6. **Fauna role**:
   - Highlights often-neglected component
   - 71% of mineralization fauna-mediated
   - Drought effects operate through fauna populations

**Unique contributions to meta-analysis**:

1. **Interaction type database**:
   - Can test if antagonism generalizes across studies
   - Framework for meta-analyzing interaction types
   - Hypothesis: Antagonism more common than appreciated

2. **Multi-variable response patterns**:
   - Not just single response variable per study
   - Can analyze consistency across N cycle components
   - Tests whether treatment effects cascade through ecosystem

3. **Temperate heathland ecosystem**:
   - Underrepresented in warming literature

4. **Methodological comparison**:
   - Isotope dilution + buried bags (with Andresen 2010)
   - Can compare lab vs. in situ approaches
   - Validates upscaling methods

**Integration recommendations**:

   - 47 variables available

   - Same site, complementary methods
   - Tests robustness of findings across approaches
   - Validates in situ vs. lab comparison

   - Both show antagonistic interactions
