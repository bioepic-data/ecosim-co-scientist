# Ma et al. 2011 - Warming and Nitrogen Addition Effects on Soil N Cycling

## Citation

**Full Citation**: Ma L-N, Lü X-T, Liu Y, Guo J-X, Zhang N-Y, Yang J-Q, Wang R-Z (2011). The Effects of Warming and Nitrogen Addition on Soil Nitrogen Cycling in a Temperate Grassland, Northeastern China. *PLoS ONE* 6(11): e27645.

**DOI**: 10.1371/journal.pone.0027645

**Study Period**: 2006-2009 (treatments began 2006, measurements 2007-2009)

**Study Duration**: 3 growing seasons of measurements

## Study Site

**Location**: Research Station of Songnen Grassland Ecosystem, Jilin Province, northeastern China (44°45'N, 123°45'E)

**Ecosystem Type**: Temperate continental grassland (Eurasian steppe)

**Soil**:
- Type: Sodic saline meadow soil
- pH: 8.2
- Soil organic matter: 3-4% in surface layer
- Texture: Not specified

**Climate**:
- Mean annual temperature: 4.9°C
- Mean annual land surface temperature: 6.2°C
- Mean annual precipitation: 410 mm (1980-2006)
- Precipitation pattern: 90% from May to October (growing season)
- **Study years were drier than average**:
  - 2007: 275.9 mm (32.7% below mean) - **dry year**
  - 2008: 384.2 mm (6.2% below mean)
  - 2009: 390 mm (4.9% below mean)

**Vegetation**:
- **Dominant species**: *Leymus chinensis* (Trin.) Tzvel. (perennial C3 grass)
- **Other species**: *Kalimeris integrifolia*, *Carex duriuscula*, *Phragmites* spp.
- Shoot heights: 30-40 cm
- Maximum litter: ~0.5 kg m⁻²

## Experimental Design

### Treatments (2×2 factorial design)

**Factorial design** testing:

1. **Warming (W)**: Infrared heating
   - Method: Infrared radiators (Kalglo Electronics MSR-2420)
   - Position: 2.25 m above plot center
   - Power: 1700 W continuous
   - **Achieved warming**: +1.8°C at 15 cm soil depth
   - Control plots: "Dummy" heaters (same shading)

2. **Nitrogen addition (N)**: 10 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹
   - Form: Aqueous ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃)
   - Application: Single pulse in early May each year
   - Water equivalent: ~2 mm rain
   - Control plots: Same water addition, no N

**Replication**:
- 4 treatments (Control, W, N, WN)
- 6 replicates per treatment
- 24 plots total
- Plot size: 2 m × 3 m
- Complete randomized block design

### Measurements

#### 1. Soil Microclimate (Continuous monitoring)

**Equipment**: ECH2O dielectric aquameter (Em50)
- Soil temperature at 15 cm depth
- Soil water content at 0-15 cm depth
- Timing: 8:00-9:00 AM, 6 times per growing season
  - Late May, mid-June, mid-July, early August, mid-September, mid-October

#### 2. Soil Nitrogen Mineralization (Lab incubation method)

**Sampling**: Same dates as microclimate measurements
- Depth: 0-15 cm
- Core sampler: 5 cm diameter × 15 cm length
- Two cores per plot, pooled
- Fresh soil (not dried) used to minimize microbial disturbance

**Incubation procedure** (aerobic):
- 10 g dry soil + 10 g acid-washed sand
- Mixed in 250 mL triangle bottle
- Adjusted to 60% water-holding capacity
- Covered with 0.01 mm plastic film
- Incubated at 25°C for 14 days
- Water added periodically to maintain moisture

**Parameters measured**:
- **Net N mineralization rate (NMR)**: Change in NH₄⁺-N + NO₃⁻-N
- **Net nitrification rate (NNR)**: Change in NO₃⁻-N
- Extraction: 2 M KCl, 1 hour shaking, centrifuge
- NH₄⁺ analysis: Steam distillation + titration
- NO₃⁻ analysis: UV photometry at 210 nm

#### 3. Microbial Biomass and Community Structure

**Microbial biomass N (MBN)**:
- Method: Chloroform fumigation-extraction
- Conversion factor (k_en): 0.45
- Calculated as difference between fumigated and unfumigated samples

**Phospholipid Fatty Acids (PLFAs)** - Microbial community structure:
- Extraction from 8 g dry soil
- Analysis: Gas Chromatograph (HP 6850) + Sherlock Microbial ID System V3.1
- **Bacterial markers**: i15:0, a15:0, i16:1c, i16:0, 16:1ω7c, i17:0, 17:1ω6c, a17:0, 17:0cy, 18:1ω7c, 18:1ω5c, 19:0cy
- **Fungal markers**: 16:1ω5c, 18:2ω6,9c, 18:1ω9c
- **Bacterial:Fungal ratio (B:F)**: Key indicator of microbial community shift

#### 4. Soil Chemistry

- **Soil organic carbon (SOC)**: Dichromate oxidation method
- **Total nitrogen (TN)**: Kjeldahl digestion with H₂SO₄
- **C:N ratio**: Calculated from SOC and TN
- **Available N**: NH₄⁺-N + NO₃⁻-N in 2 M KCl extract

#### 5. Plant Nitrogen Content

**Species**: *Leymus chinensis* (indicator species)
- Sampling: Fall of 2007, 2008, 2009
- 20 plants per plot with intact root systems
- Separated into aboveground and belowground
- Oven-dried at 70°C
- N analysis: Kjeldahl digestion

## Key Results

### Soil Microclimate Effects

**Temperature**:
- Warming increased soil temperature at 15 cm (F₁,₂₀=6.33, P<0.05)
- **W×N interaction** (F₁,₂₀=4.97, P<0.05): Warming effect significant under ambient N but not under N addition
- Seasonal pattern: One-peak (higher in summer, lower in spring/autumn)

**Soil water content**:
- 2007 (dry year) significantly drier than 2008 and 2009 (P<0.001)
- Warming decreased soil moisture (F₁,₂₀=6.74, P<0.05)
- **W×N interaction** (F₁,₂₀=5.56, P<0.05): N addition decreased moisture more under warming

### Microbial Community Responses

**Bacterial:Fungal ratio (B:F)** - Major finding:
- **N addition**: +60%, +63%, +56% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.001)
- **Warming**: +58%, +57%, +47% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.001)
- **W×N interaction significant** (P<0.001): Combined effect smaller than additive

**Fungal PLFAs**:
- **N addition**: -28%, -25.4%, -21% (P<0.001) - fungi decreased
- **Warming**: -23.2%, -24.7%, -17.4% (P<0.001) - fungi decreased
- **W×N interaction significant** (P<0.001)

**Total PLFAs** (total microbial biomass):
- **N addition**: +13.2%, +20.3%, +21.5% (P<0.01)
- **Warming**: +16.8%, +18.7%, +19.2% (P<0.01)

**Microbial biomass N (MBN)**:
- **N addition**: +13.2%, +15.7%, +16.6% (P<0.01)
- **Warming**: +17.7%, +12.5%, +16.6% (P<0.01)

### Soil Chemistry Responses

**Soil C:N ratio**:
- **N addition**: -10.2%, -9.7%, -10% (P<0.05) - soil quality improved

**Soil available N (NH₄⁺ + NO₃⁻)**:
- **No significant treatment effects** despite increased mineralization
- Interpretation: N uptake by plants and microbes balanced increased mineralization

### Plant Nitrogen Uptake

**Aboveground plant N content**:
- **N addition**: +27%, +30.4%, +29.4% (P<0.001)
- **Warming**: No main effect
- **W×N interaction** (P<0.01): Warming increased plant N only under N addition

**Belowground plant N content**:
- **N addition**: +14.2%, +13.5%, +11.2% (P<0.05)

### Nitrogen Mineralization Rates

**Net N Mineralization Rate (NMR)**:

**Main effects** (consistent across 3 years):
- **Warming**: +18%, +16.9%, +17% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.01)
- **N addition**: +12.3%, +11.8%, +11.9% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.05)

**Interaction effect - ANTAGONISTIC** (P<0.05):
- **Combined W+N**: Only +17.5% to +20.1% above control
- **Expected additive effect**: +28.7% to +30.3%
- **Difference**: ~30-40% reduction from expected additive effect

**Net Nitrification Rate (NNR)**:

**Main effects**:
- **Warming**: +14.8%, +17.6%, +18.3% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.01)
- **N addition**: +11%, +12.9%, +15.3% in 2007, 2008, 2009 (P<0.05)

**Interaction effect - ANTAGONISTIC** (P<0.05):
- **Combined W+N**: Only +16.2% to +20.2%
- **Expected additive**: +28.6% to +31.6%

**Temporal patterns**:
- Unimodal seasonal pattern (higher summer, lower spring/autumn)
- 2007 (dry year) had lower NMR and NNR than 2008/2009 (P<0.001)
- **Response to treatments consistent across years** despite precipitation differences

### Controls on N Mineralization

**Temporal controls** (stepwise regression across seasons):
- Soil temperature: Partial R²=0.43 for NMR, 0.42 for NNR (P<0.001)
- Soil water content: Partial R²=0.29 for NMR, 0.32 for NNR (P<0.01)
- **Combined**: Explained 72% (NMR) and 74% (NNR) of seasonal variation

**Spatial controls** (stepwise regression across 24 plots):

*2007*:
- B:F ratio + Total PLFAs → 58% of NMR variance
- B:F ratio + Total PLFAs → 58% of NNR variance

*2008*:
- B:F + Total PLFAs + C:N → 73% of NMR variance
- B:F + Total PLFAs → 59% of NNR variance

*2009*:
- B:F + Total PLFAs + C:N → 71% of NMR variance
- B:F + Total PLFAs + C:N → 67% of NNR variance

## Major Findings

### 1. Both Warming and N Addition Stimulate N Mineralization

**Mechanisms**:
- **Warming**: Increased microbial biomass and activity
- **N addition**: Direct fertilization effect + improved soil quality (lower C:N)
- Both effects consistent across 3 years with varying precipitation

### 2. Antagonistic Interaction Between Warming and N Addition

**Key Discovery**: Combined effects significantly less than additive prediction

**Mechanisms proposed**:

a) **Microbial community shift**:
- Combined treatment increased fungal biomass more than single treatments
- Higher fungal:bacterial ratio under W+N
- Fungi:
  - Enmesh microaggregates into macroaggregates → SOM stabilization
  - Cell walls (melanin, chitin) persist longer → slower decomposition
  - Result: Reduced decomposition rate

b) **Strong plant N uptake**:
- Aboveground plant N showed synergistic response to W+N
- Plants captured added N more effectively under warming
- Rapid decline in soil available N pool
- Reduced substrate for mineralization

### 3. Precipitation Variation Affects Magnitude But Not Response Direction

**Dry year (2007)** vs. **Wet years (2008, 2009)**:
- Lower absolute N mineralization in dry year (P<0.001)
- **But**: Treatment response percentages similar across years
- Interpretation: Warming and N effects independent of precipitation variation
- Implication: Consistent climate change responses despite weather variability

### 4. Fate of Added Nitrogen

Despite increased mineralization:
- **Soil available N pools unchanged**
- **Explanation**:
  - Plant uptake increased (aboveground +27-30%, belowground +11-14%)
  - Microbial immobilization increased (MBN +13-17%)
  - Possible gaseous losses (N₂O, N₂, NO) from denitrification
- **Conclusion**: Added N directly utilized by plants and microbes, not accumulated in soil

## Implications for EcoSIM Modeling

### This Study Provides:

**Well-controlled factorial experiment**:
- Clean separation of warming and N effects
- Quantified interaction term
- Multi-year data (inter-annual variability)
- Multiple growing season measurements (intra-annual variability)

**Mechanistic understanding**:
- Microbial community structure changes (B:F ratio)
- Plant-microbe competition for N
- Temperature and moisture co-limitation
- Precipitation-independent climate responses

**Comprehensive measurements**:
- Process rates (mineralization, nitrification)
- Microbial biomass and community structure (PLFAs)
- Plant N uptake (above and belowground)
- Soil chemistry (C:N, available N)
- Environmental drivers (temperature, moisture)

### Key Parameters for EcoSIM

**Treatment specifications**:
- Warming: +1.8°C continuous
- N addition: 10 g N m⁻² yr⁻¹ (single pulse, May)
- Precipitation variation: 276-390 mm growing season

**Validation targets**:
- NMR response to warming: +16-18%
- NMR response to N addition: +11-13%
- **Interaction effect**: -30 to -40% from additive expectation
- B:F ratio changes: +47-60%
- Fungal PLFA changes: -17 to -28%

**Process relationships**:
- NMR vs. soil temperature: R²=0.43
- NMR vs. soil moisture: R²=0.29
- NMR vs. B:F ratio: R²=0.23-0.27
- NMR vs. total PLFAs: R²=0.32-0.39

### Model Testing Opportunities:

1. **Can EcoSIM reproduce the antagonistic interaction?**
   - Is interaction emergent from plant-microbe competition?
   - Or does it require explicit parameterization?

2. **Microbial community dynamics**:
   - Does model track bacterial vs. fungal shifts?
   - Can it simulate slower fungal-dominated decomposition?

3. **Plant N uptake under warming**:
   - Does model capture enhanced uptake when N available?
   - Temperature sensitivity of root uptake kinetics?

4. **Inter-annual consistency**:
   - Can model reproduce stable treatment responses across wet/dry years?
   - Precipitation effects on magnitude but not direction?

5. **Fate of added N**:
   - Model prediction of plant vs. microbial vs. gaseous fate
   - Balance between mineralization and immobilization

## Comparison to Andresen et al. 2010

### Similarities:
- Both multi-factorial (warming + nutrient addition)
- Both temperate ecosystems
- Both found treatment interactions (though different types)
- Both ~3 years duration
- Both measured microbial biomass

### Differences:

| Feature | Ma et al. 2011 (China) | Andresen et al. 2010 (Denmark) |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Ecosystem | Grassland | Heathland |
| Soil pH | 8.2 (alkaline) | 4.6 (acidic) |
| Precipitation | 276-410 mm | 600 mm |
| Warming method | Infrared (continuous) | Nighttime curtains (passive) |
| Warming magnitude | +1.8°C | +1.5°C |
| Nutrient added | N only (10 g m⁻²) | None (but measured responses) |
| Drought treatment | No | Yes |
| CO₂ treatment | No | Yes (FACE) |
| Interaction type | **Antagonistic** (W×N) | **Synergistic/Complex** (multiple) |
| Incubation | Lab (constant 25°C) | In situ (buried bags) |
| Plant species | 1 dominant (*Leymus*) | 2 dominant (*Calluna*, *Deschampsia*) |
| Microbial analysis | PLFAs (detailed B:F) | PLFAs + biomass C:N:P |
| Key mechanism | Fungal increase + plant uptake | Species-specific, P limitation under CO₂ |

### Complementary Value:

- **Ma et al.**: Clean W×N interaction in grassland, alkaline soil
- **Andresen et al.**: Multi-factorial (W×D×CO₂) in heathland, acidic soil
- Together: Span pH gradient, ecosystem types, and climate factor combinations

## Data Quality and Limitations

### Strengths:
- Factorial design with good replication (n=6)
- Multi-year data captures inter-annual variability
- Consistent measurements across 3 years
- Multiple process measurements (rates, pools, community structure)
- Mechanistic measurements (PLFAs for community structure)
- Standardized incubation method (reduces field variability)
- Open access publication (PLoS ONE)

### Limitations:
- **Lab incubation method**:
  - Constant 25°C and 60% WHC (not field conditions)
  - Potential N mineralization, not actual field rates
  - Severs plant-soil connections
- **Single site** (no spatial replication)
- **Grassland only** (dominated by one species)
- **No direct drought treatment** (only natural precipitation variation)
- **No CO₂ treatment** (limits comparison to full factorial studies)
- **No litter decomposition measurements**
- **Seasonal measurements only** (6 per year, not continuous)
- **Plant sampling**: Only one species (*Leymus chinensis*)

### Uncertainty Sources:
- Spatial variability within plots
- Extrapolation from lab incubation to field
- Microbial community analysis limited to PLFAs (no molecular methods)
- Gaseous N losses not measured directly
- Root N content has high variability

## Relevance to Warming/Nitrogen Meta-Analysis

**High relevance**:
- **Direct test of warming × N addition interaction**
- Clean factorial design
- Multi-year dataset
- Quantifies antagonistic interaction magnitude
- Mechanistic understanding (microbial community shifts)
- Temperate grassland ecosystem (common in meta-analysis)

**Unique contributions**:
- **Alkaline soil** (pH 8.2) - rare in literature
- **Antagonistic interaction** - contrasts with additive assumptions
- **Fungal increase under combined treatment** - unexpected mechanism
- **Precipitation-independent responses** - climate robustness
- **Continental Asian grassland** - geographic diversity

**Integration with meta-analysis**:
- Provides interaction term estimate: ~-35% from additive
- Mechanistic pathway: fungal biomass → slower decomposition
- Plant uptake as N sink under warming
- Can test whether interaction direction varies by ecosystem/soil type

## Notes

- First study to document antagonistic W×N interaction in temperate grassland
- Microbial community structure (B:F ratio) emerged as key control
- Plant N uptake synergistic under W+N (opposite of soil N mineralization)
- Lab incubation method allows controlled comparison but limits field applicability
- Study emphasizes biotic controls (microbial community, plant uptake) over abiotic
- *Leymus chinensis* grasslands important for carbon storage in northeastern China
- Alkaline soil chemistry (pH 8.2) creates different nutrient dynamics than acidic systems
