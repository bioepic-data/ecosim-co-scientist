# Chapin et al. 1995 - Arctic Tundra Response to Experimental and Observed Climate Change

## Citation
Chapin, F. S., III, Shaver, G. R., Giblin, A. E., Nadelhoffer, K. J., & Laundre, J. A. (1995). Responses of arctic tundra to experimental and observed changes in climate. Ecology, 76(3), 694-711.

## Study Overview
This is a **landmark 9-year field manipulation experiment** examining how Arctic tundra responds to changes in light, temperature, and nutrients expected under global climate change. The study is particularly important because:

1. **Long duration**: 9 years (1981-1989), allowing observation of both immediate and sustained responses
2. **Multiple factors**: Simultaneously tested light, temperature, and nutrient effects
3. **Comprehensive measurements**: Biomass, production, nutrient cycling, species diversity, and environmental conditions
4. **Real climate change signal**: Control plots showed vegetation changes during the warmest decade on record
5. **Ecosystem-level perspective**: Included nonvascular plants (mosses, lichens), not just vascular plants

### Research Questions
1. How do light, temperature, and nutrients affect Arctic tundra community structure and ecosystem processes?
2. Are short-term (3-yr) responses good predictors of long-term (9-yr) changes?
3. How does species composition change with simulated climate warming?
4. What are the feedbacks between vegetation change and nutrient availability?
5. Is Arctic vegetation already responding to regional climate warming?

### Key Innovation
First long-term (9-yr) experimental study of Arctic tundra response to multiple climate change factors, with measurements comprehensive enough to detect ecosystem-level compensation and surprising evidence of ongoing climate change effects in control plots.

## Site Characteristics

### Location
- **Site**: Toolik Lake, Arctic Long-Term Ecological Research (LTER) site
- **Coordinates**: 68°38' N, 149°34' W
- **Elevation**: 760 m
- **Region**: Northern foothills of the Brooks Range, Alaska
- **Deglaciation**: 60,000-100,000 years BP (Itkillik I glaciation)
- **Tundra age**: Dominated by tussock tundra for at least 7,000 years

### Ecosystem Type
**Moist tussock tundra** - the most productive tundra type in the Low Arctic

**Vegetation composition** (approximately equal biomass of each):
- **Graminoids**: Mainly Eriophorum vaginatum (tussock cottongrass)
- **Deciduous shrubs**: Mainly Betula nana (dwarf birch)
- **Evergreen shrubs**: Mainly Ledum palustre ssp. decumbens (Labrador tea) and Vaccinium vitis-idaea (lingonberry)
- **Mosses**: Mainly Sphagnum spp., Hylocomium splendens, and Aulacomnium spp.

### Dominant Species
**Vascular plants** (6 most abundant species):
1. **Eriophorum vaginatum** (graminoid) - tussock-forming sedge, ecosystem engineer
2. **Carex bigelowii** (graminoid) - sedge in inter-tussock spaces
3. **Betula nana** (deciduous shrub) - dwarf birch
4. **Rubus chamaemorus** (deciduous forb/shrub) - cloudberry, shade-tolerant understory
5. **Ledum palustre** (evergreen shrub) - Labrador tea
6. **Vaccinium vitis-idaea** (evergreen shrub) - lingonberry

**Nonvascular plants**:
- Sphagnum mosses (characteristic of moist, oligotrophic habitats)
- Aulacomnium mosses (respond to eutrophication)
- Hylocomium splendens (feather moss)
- Foliose and fruticose lichens

**Rare species**: Mostly deciduous forbs (important for caribou and pollinators)

### Climate
- **Type**: Cool temperate to cold, humid
- **Expected changes with global warming**:
  - Warmer air and soil temperatures (3°-8°C increase predicted)
  - More rapid nutrient release from decomposition
  - Increased summer cloudiness (from sea ice melting and greater ocean evaporation)

### Historical Context
- **No recent disturbance**: No charcoal in 7,000-year peat profile (no fire history)
- **No herbivore outbreaks**: During 16-year study period
- **Regional warming**: 4°C rise in permafrost temperature in last 50 years
- **Study decade**: Warmest decade on record in the region (1982-1992)

## Experimental Design

### Overview
- **Design type**: Randomized complete block design
- **Duration**: 9 years (June 1981 - August 1989)
- **Blocks**: 4 replicate blocks
- **Plots per block**: 4 plots (5 × 20 m each)
- **Plots separated by**: 1-m buffer strips
- **Key measurement years**: 1983 (3-year response) and 1989 (9-year response)
- **Control measurements**: 1982, 1984, 1991, 1992 (Eriophorum and Betula only in latter two)

### Treatments

**1. Control (C)**
- Unmanipulated tundra
- Natural conditions
- 100 m² area per plot

**2. Nutrient Addition (N)**
- **N addition**: 10 g/m² as NH₄NO₃ each spring after snowmelt
- **P addition**: 5 g/m² as P₂O₅ each spring after snowmelt
- **Fertilizer context**:
  - N addition = 4× annual N requirement for aboveground vascular production
  - P addition = 20× annual P requirement
  - Applied annually for 9 years
- 100 m² area per plot

**3. Elevated Temperature (T)**
- **Method**: Transparent greenhouse over tundra
- **Design**: A-shaped wooden frame covered with 0.15-mm (6 mil) clear plastic sheeting
- **Dimensions**: 10 cm high at sides, 1 m high in center
- **Ventilation**: Uneven microtopography allowed air circulation at base (open greenhouse design)
- **Seasonal deployment**: Placed after snowmelt each spring, removed each autumn (1981-1989)
- **Plastic maintenance**: Replaced annually
- **Area**: 12 m²

**4. Nutrient × Temperature (N×T)**
- Heated greenhouse + nutrient addition
- Both fertilized and non-fertilized greenhouses in temperature plot
- Combined treatments

**5. Light Attenuation (L)**
- **Method**: Shade cloth over tundra
- **Frame**: Identical to greenhouse but covered with optically neutral shade cloth
- **Light reduction**: 50% of incoming photosynthetically active radiation (400-700 nm)
- **Confirmation**: Measured with LiCor quantum sensor
- **Seasonal deployment**: Same as greenhouse
- **Area**: 12 m²
- Single unfertilized shade frame per light plot

### Statistical Design
**Pretreatment verification** (June 1991):
- Two-way ANOVA (randomized complete block)
- Scheffé pairwise comparison of means
- Friedman nonparametric two-way ANOVA
- Each sampling date analyzed separately
- **Result**: No preexisting differences confirmed (one exception: soil temp at 4 cm, significant on only one date, likely random)

**Post-treatment analysis**:
- Two-way nested ANOVA for nutrient and temperature effects
- Separate one-way nested ANOVA for light effects
- Quadrats nested within blocks
- Harvests as repeated measures
- Both log-transformed (multiplicative model) and untransformed (additive model) data analyzed
- Significance level: α = 0.05

### Sampling Design
**Biomass harvests**:
- 5 randomly selected 20 × 20 cm quadrats per plot (n = 20 per treatment)
- **Snow melt harvest**: May 21-22, 1983 (to measure overwintering buds)
- **Peak biomass harvests**: Late July/early August 1983 and 1989
- **Nonvascular plant harvest**: Early June 1984 (before moss growth began; omitted from 1983)

**Nested sampling areas**:
- 5 × 5 m experimental area nested within each 6 × 6 m plot for temperature and light treatments
- Avoids edge effects where heating was suboptimal
- No difference in variance between different-sized plots

## Measurements

### Environmental Variables

**Continuous measurements** (June 11 - July 13, 1982):
- Photosynthetically active radiation (400-700 nm): LiCor quantum sensors
- Air temperature (50 cm above ground): Shaded thermocouple
- Soil temperature (10 cm depth in moss mats between tussocks): Thermocouples
- Tussock temperature (10 cm depth within tussocks): Thermocouples (control and temperature plots only)
- Surface temperature (litter on top of tussocks): Thermocouples
- **Frequency**: Every minute, hourly averages recorded on Campbell CR5 datalogger
- **Analysis**: Daily mean ± SE calculated; hourly patterns for 5 cloudiest and 5 sunniest days

**Soil physical properties** (August 1989):
- **Thaw depth**: 4 random points per plot, probe pushed from moss surface to frozen soil
- **Soil moisture**: Gravimetric (oven-dry), 4 samples per plot, 10 cm beneath moss mat

**CO₂ concentration** (August 1991):
- Measured 20 cm above canopy in 5 warmed and 5 control plots
- LiCor 6200 portable infrared gas analyzer
- Three times during one clear day (to span daily irradiance range)
- Comparison to 1980 data (gas chromatograph)

**Relative humidity** (1991):
- Hourly measurements in one heated and one control plot
- Campbell CR10 datalogger

### Biomass Measurements

**Vascular plants** - separated by:
- **Species**
- **Tissue categories**:
  - Previous years' biomass: old belowground stem, old aboveground stem, old leaves
  - Current year's biomass: new aboveground stem, new leaves, inflorescences, new rhizome buds (graminoids only)
- **Graminoid details**: Leaves separated into blade and sheath; inflorescences into current and next year's buds
- **Live vs. senescent**: Senescent leaves/tips that were alive earlier that season included as live based on color
- **Note**: Roots NOT harvested due to difficulty in organic soils
- All samples oven-dried at 65°C and weighed

**Nonvascular plants** (1984 and 1989):
- **Mosses**: Separated by genus (Sphagnum, Aulacomnium, Hylocomium)
  - For Hylocomium and Aulacomnium: Separated 1983 growth from previous years' biomass
  - New growth was 20 ± 2% of green biomass in both species (all treatments except nutrient)
  - Nutrient treatment: 30 ± 4% for Hylocomium, 37 ± 2% for Aulacomnium
- **Lichens**: Separated by group (foliose and fruticose)
- **Sampling**: 10 6.5-cm² random quadrats per plot (1984 only, before moss growth began)

**Secondary stem growth** (1985):
- Measured in Salix pulchra and Ledum palustre (control and nutrient plots only)
- Method: Regressions of ln(mass per unit length) vs. age of stem segments
- Assumed Betula had same annual % increase as Salix (Betula stems couldn't be aged)
- Evergreens other than Ledum: Insignificant secondary growth
- Belowground stems: Insignificant secondary growth for all species

### Production Calculations

**Definitions**:
- **Aboveground plant parts**: All sampled tissues except belowground stems and new rhizome buds (includes graminoid leaf sheaths)
- **Current year's aboveground biomass**: Current year's leaves + stems + inflorescences (includes overwintering buds at snowmelt)
- **Apical aboveground growth**: Increase in new aboveground biomass between snow melt and maximum aboveground biomass
- **Aboveground NPP**: Apical aboveground growth + stem secondary growth

**Assumptions**:
- Same proportional increase in new biomass between snowmelt and peak for 1989 as measured in 1983
- Secondary stem growth in N×T plots = same proportion of old-stem biomass as in N plots
- Secondary stem growth in T and L plots = same proportion as in C plots
- Stem secondary growth effects were small (0-2% difference) compared to large variability in old-stem biomass

**Note on NPP analysis**:
- Could not analyze NPP statistically (calculated from components measured in different samples)
- Interpreted cautiously, assuming variability similar to statistically analyzed parameters

### Nutrient Measurements

**Soil nutrients** (August 1989):
- **Sampling**: Upper 5 cm of organic horizon + upper 5 cm of mineral horizon
- **Extractable NH₄⁺ and NO₃⁻**: 2 mol/L KCl extraction
- **Extractable PO₄³⁻**: 0.025 mol/L HCl extraction
- **Analysis**: Colorimetry by autoanalyzer

**Resin bag measurements** (Summer 1988 and winters 1987-1988, 1988-1989):
- **Method**: Anion and cation exchange resins in nylon stocking bags
- **Placement**: 5 cm beneath top of organic horizon
- **Extraction**: 2 mol/L KCl
- **Analysis**: NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻, and PO₄³⁻ by colorimetry (autoanalyzer)
- **Purpose**: Measure plant-available nutrients over time

**Plant tissue nutrients** (1983 and 1989):
- **1983 sampling**: Each tissue type of each species analyzed separately
- **1989 sampling**: Pooled by growth form; new vs. old growth separated within vascular growth forms
- **Sample pooling**: 5 quadrats per plot lumped together
- **Preparation**: Ground in Wiley mill, digested in selenous-sulfuric acid
- **Analysis**:
  - N: Colorimetry by autoanalyzer (nitroferricyanide procedure)
  - P: Colorimetry by autoanalyzer (phosphomolybdate procedure)
  - K, Ca, Mg (1983 only): Atomic absorption (Ca and Mg with lanthanum chloride to reduce interference)

**Nutrient pools**: Biomass × nutrient concentration for each tissue type

**Nutrient requirement**: Pool of nutrients in apical aboveground growth (uptake + stored reserves)

## Results

### Environmental Responses

**Temperature treatment effects**:
- **Air temperature (daily mean)**: +3.5°C (11.2 → 14.7°C) ***P < 0.01***
- **Air temperature (daily maximum)**: +10.0°C (17.4 → 27.4°C) ***P < 0.001***
- **Air temperature (daily minimum)**: No change (4.4 → 3.8°C, NS) - no nocturnal warming
- **Surface temperature**: +5.0°C (12.0 → 17.0°C) ***P < 0.001***
- **Soil temperature (moss mat)**: +2.2°C (3.6 → 5.8°C) ***P < 0.001***
- **Tussock temperature**: +2.8°C (6.3 → 9.1°C) **P < 0.01**
- **Thaw depth**: +4.3 cm (38.3 → 42.6 cm) ***P < 0.001**
- **Irradiance**: -32% (46.0 → 31.5 mol·m⁻²·d⁻¹) ***P < 0.001*** - major confounding effect
- **Relative humidity**: No change (68.1 → 67.0%, NS)
- **CO₂ concentration**: No change (332 → 334 µL/L in 1980; 343 → 341 µL/L in 1991, NS)

**Daily patterns** (clear vs. cloudy days):
- Temperature increase greater on sunny days than cloudy days
- Nocturnal air temperature: No warming effect
- Ventilation at greenhouse base prevented humidity and CO₂ artifacts

**Nutrient treatment effects**:
- **Thaw depth**: Slight reduction (38.3 → 31.7 cm, P < 0.1) - likely from denser canopy and drier soils
- **Soil moisture (1989)**: Reduced (479 → 432% dry mass, *P < 0.05*) - from increased transpiration

**Light treatment effects**:
- **Irradiance**: -50% (46.0 → 23.0 mol·m⁻²·d⁻¹) as intended
- **Thaw depth**: Tendency to reduce (38.3 → 31.6 cm, P < 0.1)
- **Temperature**: No change
- **Soil moisture**: No change

**Context - exceeded natural temperature range**:
- Heated plots achieved soil temperatures **higher than any measured at Harvard Forest during previous 3 years**
- Extended temperature range beyond natural variability

**Key patterns**:
1. **Nutrient addition**: Massively increased all nutrients (10-100× increases)
2. **Leaching**: Nutrients leached into mineral soil in fertilized plots (exceeded plant demand)

   - Reflects plant uptake, net mineralization, and deployment duration differences
4. **Temperature effect**: Consistently increased NH₄⁺ availability in organic horizon and on resins
   - Suggests enhanced mineralization
   - Winter effect surprising (greenhouses removed) → indicates species composition/litter quality changes
5. **Light effect**: Increased NH₄⁺ in organic soils and tended to increase winter resin nutrients
   - Reflects reduced plant uptake as growth became light-limited

**Statistical significance** (Total biomass):
- Nutrient effect: **P < 0.01** (harvest), *P < 0.05* (N×harvest interaction)
- Temperature effect: *P < 0.05*
- Light effect: ***P < 0.001*** (L), *P < 0.05* (L×harvest)
- N×T interaction: *P < 0.05*

**Key biomass patterns**:

**3-year responses (1983)**:
- **Nutrient**: Increased graminoids (Eriophorum +30%, Carex +79%), deciduous shrubs (+63%), mosses (+47%)
- **Temperature**: Little change in total or by growth form
- **Light**: Reduced all growth forms
- **N×T**: Similar to nutrient effect

**9-year responses (1989)**:
- **Nutrient**: Deciduous shrubs dominated (+175% vs. control; Betula +160%)
  - Graminoids declined (-10% vs. control; Eriophorum +29% vs. control but -65% vs. 1983)
  - Evergreens declined dramatically (-49% vs. control; Ledum -36%, Vaccinium -68%)
  - Nonvascular plants nearly eliminated (-84% vs. control)
- **Temperature**: Small total decline (-4%)
  - Eriophorum increased (+96% vs. control)
  - Carex declined (-59% vs. control)
  - Ledum increased (+46% vs. control)
  - Vaccinium increased (+24% vs. control)
  - Nonvascular plants declined (-52% vs. control)
- **N×T**: Dramatic Betula dominance (3× control, +179%)
  - All other species declined or nearly eliminated
  - Eriophorum crashed (-90% vs. control, -95% vs. 1983)
  - Mosses and lichens nearly 100% eliminated (covered by Betula litter)
  - Most extreme vegetation change
- **Light**: All species declined
  - Total biomass -43% vs. control
  - Rubus (shade-tolerant) tended to increase (reverse of other species)
  - Nonvascular plants -46% vs. control (less than N×T -67% reduction)

**Within-growth-form patterns**:
- **Nutrients and light**: All species of same growth form responded similarly
  - Both deciduous shrubs stimulated by nutrients
  - Both evergreens inhibited by nutrients
- **Temperature**: Species within growth form showed contrasting responses
  - Eriophorum increased, Carex decreased (both graminoids)
  - No consistent pattern by growth form

**Ecosystem-level compensation**:
- Large biomass changes in individual species
- Relatively small changes in total ecosystem biomass (coefficient of variation = 20% across treatments)
- Individual species CV averaged 67 ± 5%
- Compensation through redistribution among species and growth forms

   - Increased graminoid production
   - Reduced nonvascular production (especially at 9 yr)
   - Strong N×T interaction (temperature reduced production in fertilized plots)
4. **Light effect**: Reduced all growth forms except deciduous shrubs
5. **Ecosystem compensation**: CV of NPP across treatments (33%) less than individual species CV (87 ± 12%)

### Species Richness and Diversity

**Vascular plant species per plot** (estimated from dominance-diversity curves):

| Treatment | 1983 species | 1989 species | Loss |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|------|
| Control | ~14-15 | ~13-14 | ~1 (7%) |
| Nutrient | ~12-13 | ~9-10 | ~3-4 (30%) |
| Temperature | ~14-15 | ~10-11 | ~4 (30%) |
| N×T | ~12-13 | ~6-7 | ~6 (50%) |
| Light | ~14-15 | ~13-14 | ~1 (7%) |

**Dominance patterns** (% of community by growth form):
- **Control**: 2 evergreen, 2 deciduous, 2 graminoid species among top 6
- **High diversity maintained**: Growth form diversity high in control
- **Nutrient/N×T**: Betula dominated, other species declined dramatically

**Species lost** (primarily):
- **Understory forbs** (deciduous, important for caribou nutrition and pollinators)
- **Mosses** (critical for soil thermal regime)
- **Rare species** (least abundant third of community)

**Key diversity patterns**:
1. **30-50% species loss** in temperature and nutrient treatments after 9 years
2. **No gain of initially rare species** in any treatment (no immigration)
3. **Loss disproportionate in forbs** and mosses (functional groups with ecosystem importance)
4. **Increased dominance**: 4 initially most abundant species became more dominant in temperature treatment
5. **Light effect**: Decreased biomass of all species (most strongly dominant species, least strongly rare species)
6. **Mechanism**: Competitive exclusion by rapidly growing species (especially Betula in N×T)

**Ecological consequences of diversity loss**:
- **Forbs**: Nutritionally important for caribou lactation, major food for bumblebees and pollinators
- **Lichens**: Critical for caribou over-winter nutrition
- **Mosses**: Strongly influence soil thermal regime (insulation)
- Disproportionate ecosystem effects despite being rare species

**Nutrient accumulation efficiency (first 3 years)**:
- **N**: 62% of applied N accumulated in vegetation (from 10 g/m² × 3 yr = 30 g/m² applied)
- **P**: 18% of applied P accumulated in vegetation (from 5 g/m² × 3 yr = 15 g/m² applied)
- **Mechanism**: Strong nutrient limitation + complete nonvascular plant cover absorbing surface-applied nutrients

**Nutrient redistribution patterns**:

**3-year responses (1983)**:
- **All growth forms** increased N and P pools with nutrient addition
- **Mosses**: Largest response (direct contact with fertilizer)
- **Evergreens**: Smallest response (slow nutrient uptake rates)
- **Even declining species** increased nutrient pools initially (e.g., evergreens)

**9-year responses (1989)**:
- **Deciduous shrubs**: Dominated nutrient pools in fertilized plots
- **Nonvascular plants**: N and P pools declined >50% in temperature treatment vs. control
  - Nutrients transferred from nonvascular to vascular plants
- **N×T treatment**: Lower total N and P pools than nutrient treatment (32% and 38% less)
  - Despite higher soil nutrient availability
  - Reflects reduced uptake by collapsed Eriophorum and moss populations

**Cations (K, Ca, Mg) - 1983 only**:

| Cation | Control (g/m²) | Nutrient (g/m²) | % Increase | Notes |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|------------|-------|
| K | 2.0 | 2.6 | +29% | Graminoids & deciduous contributed |
| Ca | 2.8 | 4.8 | +73% | Mosses contributed strongly |
| Mg | 1.5 | 2.0 | +36% | Graminoids & deciduous contributed |

- **Not in fertilizer**, yet increased dramatically
- Exceeds 16% biomass increase
- Demonstrates **demand-driven uptake** (not just diffusion)
- Plants maintain relatively constant N:P:cation ratios (similar to Redfield ratio in marine algae)

**Nutrient requirement (annual uptake, g·m⁻²·yr⁻¹) - 1983**:

| Nutrient | Control | Nutrient | Temperature | N×T | Light |

| **N uptake** | 2.3 | 6.7 | 2.8 | 4.7 | - |

**Key uptake patterns**:
- **Nutrient requirement >> standing stocks** (reflects high turnover)
- **Nutrient addition**: Tripled N requirement, 7-8× P requirement (graminoids & deciduous)
- **Temperature effect**: +13-21% increase in N and P requirement (despite no change in standing stocks)
  - Suggests enhanced mineralization
  - Most pronounced in vascular plants
- **Light attenuation**: Tended to reduce N uptake by vascular plants (9-yr)
  - Explains increased exchangeable ammonium in shade treatment

## Major Findings

### 1. Arctic Vegetation is Sensitive to Simulated Climate Change
- Multiple ecosystem properties changed in <decade:
  - Nutrient availability
  - Growth-form composition
  - Biomass
  - NPP
  - Species richness (30-50% decline)
- Changes occurred **entirely through shifts in resident species** (no immigration)

### 2. Short-term Responses are Poor Predictors of Long-term Changes
- 3-year responses differed substantially from 9-year responses
- Examples:
  - Graminoids and mosses initially increased with nutrients (3 yr), then declined dramatically (9 yr)
  - Temperature effects on NPP and nutrients took >3 years to develop
- **Implication**: Need long-term studies to understand climate change impacts

### 3. Long-term Responses Match Vegetation Gradients
- 9-year experimental responses corresponded to natural vegetation distributions along environmental gradients
- High-fertility sites: Deciduous shrub dominance
- Low-fertility sites: Evergreen shrub dominance
- **Implication**: Experiments that run long enough converge on natural patterns

### 4. Temperature Effects are Indirect (via Nutrient Availability)
- Direct temperature effects on NPP were small or absent in short term (3 yr)
- Major temperature effect: **Increased nutrient availability** (enhanced mineralization)
- After 9 years:
  - Temperature consistently increased nutrient accumulation on exchange resins
  - Greatest effect during winter (greenhouses removed) → indicates litter quality/species composition changes
  - Nutrients transferred from nonvascular to vascular plants
- **Mechanism**: Temperature → species composition → litter quality → decomposition → nutrient availability

### 5. Light Became Limiting After Nutrient Addition
- Light attenuation reduced biomass and NPP of all growth forms (except shade-tolerant Rubus)
- After 9 years in nutrient treatment:
  - System switched from nutrient-limited to light-limited
  - Increased nutrient availability in soils (reduced plant uptake)
- **Implication**: Sequential limitation; resolving one constraint reveals another

### 6. Ecosystem-level Compensation (Buffering)
- Large opposing responses by individual species
- **Relatively stable ecosystem properties**:
  - Total biomass CV = 20% across treatments
  - Individual species biomass CV = 67 ± 5%
  - Total NPP CV = 33% across treatments
  - Individual species NPP CV = 87 ± 12%
- **Mechanism**:
  - Resources redistributed among species
  - Replacement of slow-turnover species (mosses, evergreens) by fast-turnover species (deciduous)
- **Implication**: Ecosystem-level properties less sensitive to climate change than individual species

### 7. Growth Form Predicts Resource Response, Not Temperature Response
- **Nutrients and light**: All species within growth form responded similarly
  - Both deciduous shrubs stimulated by nutrients
  - Both evergreen shrubs inhibited by nutrients
  - All species reduced by light attenuation (except Rubus)
- **Temperature**: No consistent pattern within growth forms
  - Eriophorum increased, Carex decreased (both graminoids)
  - Species-specific temperature responses
- **Implication**: Functional types definable for resource responses, but not direct climate responses

### 8. Nutrient Addition Drove Major Composition Change
- **3-year response**: Graminoids, mosses, deciduous shrubs increased
- **9-year response**: Deciduous shrubs (especially Betula) dominated; other growth forms declined
- **Mechanism**:
  - Betula canopy shaded out understory
  - Betula litter covered mosses completely (physical smothering)
  - Positive feedback: Betula litter → faster decomposition → more nutrients → more Betula
- **30-50% species loss** in nutrient and N×T treatments

### 9. Temperature Enhanced Shrub Production, Reduced Nonvascular Production
- Vascular shrubs (both deciduous and evergreen): Production increased with temperature
- Nonvascular plants: Production declined (especially at 9 yr: -59% vs. control)
- **Shrubification**: Temperature favored vascular over nonvascular plants

### 10. Species Richness Declined Dramatically with Warming
- **30-50% species loss** in temperature and N×T treatments after 9 years
- **No rare species gained dominance** (no "winners" from initially rare pool)
- **Disproportionate losses**:
  - **Forbs**: Important for caribou (lactation period) and pollinators
  - **Mosses**: Maintain soil thermal regime
  - **Lichens**: Critical for caribou winter nutrition
- **Mechanism**: Competitive exclusion by rapidly growing dominants

### 11. Efficient Nutrient Accumulation by Tundra Vegetation
- **N**: 62% of applied N accumulated in vegetation (first 3 years)
- **P**: 18% of applied P accumulated in vegetation (first 3 years)
- Much more efficient than agricultural crops
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Strong nutrient limitation of vascular plants
  - Nearly complete nonvascular plant cover absorbing surface-applied nutrients

### 12. Demand-driven Nutrient Uptake
- Cations (K, Ca, Mg) increased 29-73% despite not being in fertilizer
- Increase exceeded biomass increase (16%)
- **Implication**: Nutrient uptake not just diffusion; plants actively regulate uptake to maintain stoichiometry

### 13. Evidence of Ongoing Regional Climate Warming Effects
- **Control plots showed large vegetation changes** during 1982-1992 (warmest decade on record):
  - Eriophorum: 70% decline
  - Betula: +91% increase (highest in warmest year, 1989)
- **Changes match experimental predictions** for warmer, more fertile conditions
- **Changes match Holocene pollen patterns**: Decreased sedge pollen during warm periods
- **Site history** makes recent disturbance or succession unlikely explanation
- **Implication**: Regional climatic warming may already be altering Arctic tundra composition

### 14. Multiple Environmental Stresses Interact
- **N×T interaction**: Temperature reduced NPP in fertilized plots (counterintuitive)
- **Mechanism**: Betula grew so rapidly it shaded out other species completely
  - More extreme than either treatment alone
  - Demonstrates non-additive responses to multiple stressors

### 15. Indirect Effects as Important as Direct Effects
- **Temperature treatment**: Reduced irradiance by 32% (unintended but realistic)
- **Nutrient treatment**: Reduced soil moisture and thaw depth (from increased transpiration and denser canopy)
- **Temperature treatment**: Increased nutrient availability (from enhanced mineralization)
- **Light treatment**: Increased soil nutrient availability (from reduced plant uptake)
- **Implication**: Manipulations create realistic complexes of changes; single-factor interpretations problematic

## Relevance to EcoSIM Modeling

### Direct Parameterization Opportunities

**1. Temperature effects on decomposition/mineralization**:
- Temperature increased nutrient availability (enhanced mineralization)
- Effect stronger in winter (suggests litter quality changes)
- Can parameterize temperature-nutrient availability feedbacks
- Test whether litter quality changes drive long-term mineralization responses

**2. Light effects on production**:
- 50% light reduction → 43% total biomass reduction after 9 yr
- All species declined (except shade-tolerant Rubus)
- Can calibrate light limitation functions for Arctic species
- Test transition from nutrient to light limitation

**3. Nutrient effects on species composition**:
- Deciduous shrubs respond positively to nutrients
- Evergreen shrubs respond negatively
- Graminoids show intermediate/variable response
- Nonvascular plants initially positive, then negative (shading)
- Can parameterize growth form-specific nutrient responses

**4. Production:biomass ratios by growth form**:
- Deciduous shrubs: High production:biomass (fast turnover)
- Evergreen shrubs: Low production:biomass (slow turnover)
- Data available for calibrating turnover rates

**5. Nutrient requirement vs. standing stocks**:
- Nutrient requirement (annual uptake) >> standing stocks
- Reflects high turnover in productive systems
- Can calibrate nutrient demand functions

**6. Nutrient use efficiency**:
- 62% of applied N accumulated (first 3 yr)
- 18% of applied P accumulated (first 3 yr)
- Higher efficiency than agricultural systems
- Can parameterize uptake efficiency

**7. Stoichiometric regulation**:
- Plants maintain N:P:cation ratios
- Cations increase even without fertilizer addition
- Can implement stoichiometric constraints

### Model Testing Opportunities

**1. Short-term vs. long-term responses**:
- Does EcoSIM predict that 3-yr responses differ from 9-yr responses?
- Initial increases followed by declines (e.g., graminoids, mosses)?
- Test whether model captures lag times and acclimation

**2. Ecosystem-level compensation**:
- Does EcoSIM show stable total NPP despite large species shifts?
- Test compensation mechanisms (resource redistribution)
- Coefficient of variation in total vs. species-level properties

**3. Sequential limitation**:
- Does model switch from nutrient to light limitation with fertilization?
- Increasing soil nutrient availability as plants become light-limited?
- Test hierarchical limitation framework

**4. Positive feedbacks**:
- Betula → faster decomposition → more nutrients → more Betula
- Does EcoSIM capture litter quality-decomposition-nutrient feedback?
- Test whether model predicts accelerating dominance shifts

**5. Temperature-nutrient interactions**:
- Does warming increase nutrient availability in model?
- Indirect effects via species composition and litter quality?
- Test time lag for temperature effects to manifest

**6. Nonvascular plant dynamics**:
- EcoSIM includes moss component
- Can test moss responses to nutrients (initial increase, then shading decline)
- Temperature effects (decline with warming)
- Importance of mosses for soil thermal regime

**7. Species diversity patterns**:
- Does model predict competitive exclusion with environmental change?
- Loss of rare species?
- No gain of initially rare species?

**8. Growth form differences**:
- Test whether model reproduces deciduous vs. evergreen responses
- High-nutrient sites: Deciduous dominance
- Low-nutrient sites: Evergreen dominance

### Process Understanding Contributions

**1. Indirect vs. direct temperature effects**:
- Study shows temperature effects are primarily indirect (via nutrients)
- Direct temperature effects small in short term
- Important for modeling climate change: Must include nutrient cycling

**2. Resource vs. climate responses**:
- Growth forms respond predictably to resources (nutrients, light)
- Growth forms do NOT respond predictably to temperature
- Implication: Models should emphasize resource availability changes

**3. Time scales of response**:
- Immediate responses (3 yr) differ from equilibrium responses (9 yr)
- Feedbacks develop over years (litter quality → decomposition → nutrients)
- Models need realistic time constants

**4. Importance of nonvascular plants**:
- Mosses and lichens comprise ~30% of biomass
- Strongly influence nutrient cycling and soil temperature
- Essential to include in models

**5. Shading as mechanism**:
- Canopy shading drove major composition changes
- Betula litter physically smothered mosses
- Models need light competition mechanisms

**6. Stoichiometric constraints**:
- Plants maintain elemental ratios
- Uptake of non-limiting elements increases with productivity
- Models should implement stoichiometry

### Limitations for Modeling

**1. No root measurements**:
- Roots are 20% of biomass, 35% of production
- Allocation patterns unknown
- Cannot validate belowground C allocation

**2. Single site**:
- Toolik Lake only
- One tundra type (moist tussock)
- Limited generalizability
- Need multi-site validation

**3. Artifacts and confounding**:
- Temperature treatment reduced light by 32%
- Difficult to separate temperature from light effects
- Need to model combined effects

**4. No CO₂ manipulation**:
- Study manipulated temperature and nutrients but not CO₂
- Cannot test CO₂ × temperature × nutrient interactions
- Need complementary studies

**5. No herbivory measurements**:
- Caribou and other herbivores important
- Vegetation changes affect animal nutrition
- Cannot validate trophic interactions

**6. No migration**:
- All responses from resident species
- No new species colonization
- Cannot test dispersal limitations

**7. NPP not statistically analyzable**:
- Calculated from components measured separately
- Higher uncertainty than biomass
- Limits quantitative model validation

### Key Insights for Model Development

**1. Long-term feedbacks essential**:
- Short-term responses misleading
- Litter quality → decomposition → nutrients → species composition
- Models must include these feedbacks

**2. Multiple limiting factors**:
- Sequential limitation (nutrients → light)
- Models need hierarchical resource limitation framework

**3. Ecosystem buffering mechanisms**:
- Large species changes, small ecosystem changes
- Resource redistribution among species
- Models should capture compensation

**4. Growth form is useful functional classification**:
- For resource responses (nutrients, light)
- NOT for direct temperature responses
- Models should use growth forms for some processes, species for others

**5. Indirect climate effects dominate**:
- Temperature → nutrient availability → species composition
- Direct temperature effects small
- Models must emphasize indirect pathways

**6. Time constants vary by process**:
- Production responds in 1 year
- Biomass responds in 3-5 years
- Soil processes respond in 5-9 years
- Models need realistic lag times

**7. Nonlinear responses to multiple stressors**:
- N×T more extreme than either alone
- Non-additive interactions common
- Models must test interaction terms

## Conclusions from Authors

### Main Conclusions

1. **Arctic vegetation is sensitive to climatic change**:
   - Nutrient availability, growth-form composition, NPP, and species richness changed in <decade
   - Changes driven entirely by shifts in resident species (no immigration)

2. **Short-term responses are poor predictors of long-term changes**:
   - 3-year responses differed substantially from 9-year responses
   - Long-term responses matched natural vegetation gradients more closely
   - Need long-term studies to understand climate change impacts

3. **Temperature effects are primarily indirect**:
   - Major effect of elevated temperature: Increased nutrient availability (enhanced mineralization)
   - Direct temperature effects on production small or absent in short term
   - After 9 years, temperature increased nutrient transfer from nonvascular to vascular plants

4. **Nutrient availability drives vegetation change**:
   - Nutrient addition caused largest vegetation changes
   - Deciduous shrubs (Betula) dominated high-nutrient treatments
   - Evergreen shrubs and nonvascular plants declined
   - Consistent with natural vegetation ordinations (high-fertility = deciduous; low-fertility = evergreen)

5. **Light limitation follows nutrient addition**:
   - Light attenuation reduced biomass of all growth forms
   - After 9 years with nutrients, system switched from nutrient to light limitation
   - Increased soil nutrient availability (reduced plant uptake)

6. **Ecosystem-level compensation buffers change**:
   - Large opposing responses by individual species
   - Relatively stable total biomass and NPP at ecosystem level
   - Compensation through resource redistribution and species replacement

7. **Growth form predicts resource response, not temperature response**:
   - Species within growth form responded similarly to nutrients and light
   - Species within growth form showed individualistic responses to temperature
   - Functional types useful for some processes, not others

8. **Species richness declined dramatically**:
   - 30-50% species loss in temperature and nutrient treatments after 9 years
   - Loss disproportionate in forbs (caribou food, pollinator plants) and mosses (soil thermal regulation)
   - No initially rare species gained dominance

9. **Evidence suggests regional warming is already altering vegetation**:
   - Control plots showed large changes during warmest decade on record
   - Eriophorum declined 70%, Betula increased 91%
   - Changes matched experimental predictions and Holocene pollen patterns
   - Regional climatic warming may already be changing Alaskan Arctic tundra

### Implications

**For ecosystem models**:
- Must include long-term feedbacks (litter quality → decomposition → nutrients → species)
- Should emphasize indirect climate effects (temperature → nutrients → vegetation)
- Need to capture ecosystem-level compensation mechanisms
- Functional types appropriate for resource responses, less so for direct climate responses

**For conservation**:
- Arctic vegetation may be more sensitive to climate change than previously thought
- Diversity losses can occur rapidly (<decade)
- Loss of functionally important species (forbs, mosses, lichens)
- Already-observable changes suggest warming impacts are underway

**For climate change predictions**:
- Short-term experiments mislead about long-term responses
- Nutrient cycling feedbacks critical for predicting vegetation change
- Ecosystem properties may be buffered despite large species changes
- Need to consider sequential limitation (nutrients → light)
